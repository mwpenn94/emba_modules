How to Use This Manual

This manual covers the foundational insurance concepts that apply across all lines of insurance — life, health, property, and casualty. It is designed for individuals preparing for general insurance licensing examinations or seeking a comprehensive understanding of insurance principles. The concepts covered here form the foundation for the more specialized Life & Health and Property & Casualty manuals in this library.

Insurance is a mechanism for transferring risk from an individual or entity (the insured) to an insurance company (the insurer) in exchange for a premium. The law of large numbers allows insurers to predict aggregate losses with reasonable accuracy by pooling many similar, independent risks. The larger the pool, the more predictable the losses become. This predictability allows insurers to set premiums that are sufficient to pay claims, cover operating expenses, and generate a reasonable profit.

Six principles govern insurance: (1) Insurable interest — the insured must have a financial stake in the subject of insurance. (2) Indemnity — the insured should be restored to their pre-loss financial position, not profit from a loss (applies to property and health, not life insurance). (3) Utmost good faith — both parties must deal honestly; the applicant must disclose all material facts. (4) Subrogation — after paying a claim, the insurer can pursue the responsible third party. (5) Contribution — when multiple policies cover the same loss, each insurer pays its proportional share. (6) Proximate cause — the insurer is liable only for losses proximately caused by a covered peril.

Risks are classified as pure risk (possibility of loss only, insurable) vs. speculative risk (possibility of gain or loss, generally not insurable). Insurable risks must be: (1) due to chance, (2) definite and measurable, (3) statistically predictable, (4) not catastrophic to the insurer, (5) economically feasible to insure, and (6) the loss must represent a hardship to the insured. Hazards increase the likelihood or severity of loss: physical hazards (icy roads), moral hazards (dishonesty, intent to cause loss), and morale hazards (carelessness due to having insurance).

An insurance policy is a legal contract requiring four elements: offer and acceptance (the application is the offer; the insurer's issuance of the policy is the acceptance), consideration (the premium paid by the insured and the promise to pay claims by the insurer), legal purpose (the contract must not violate public policy), and competent parties (both parties must have legal capacity). Insurance contracts are contracts of adhesion (written by the insurer, accepted or rejected by the insured — ambiguities are interpreted in favor of the insured), aleatory contracts (the values exchanged are unequal and depend on an uncertain event), unilateral contracts (only the insurer makes a legally enforceable promise), and conditional contracts (the insurer's obligation to pay is conditioned on the insured meeting policy conditions).

Policy components include: declarations (who, what, when, where, how much), insuring agreement (the insurer's promise to pay), conditions (obligations of both parties), exclusions (what is not covered), and endorsements/riders (modifications to the standard policy). Common policy conditions include: duty to report losses promptly, duty to cooperate with the insurer's investigation, duty to protect property from further damage, and duty to provide proof of loss. Failure to comply with conditions can result in denial of the claim.

Stock insurance companies are owned by shareholders and operated for profit. Mutual insurance companies are owned by policyholders and may pay dividends (return of premium) to policyholders. Reciprocal exchanges are unincorporated groups of individuals who insure each other. Lloyd's of London is a marketplace where syndicates of underwriters accept risk. Fraternal benefit societies are nonprofit organizations that provide insurance to their members.

Insurance distribution channels include: independent agents (represent multiple insurers, own their book of business), captive/exclusive agents (represent one insurer, the insurer owns the book of business), direct writers (sell directly to consumers without agents), and brokers (represent the insured, not the insurer, and shop for the best coverage and price). Managing general agents (MGAs) have underwriting authority from insurers and can bind coverage, issue policies, and settle claims on behalf of the insurer.

Underwriting is the process of evaluating and selecting risks to determine whether to issue a policy and at what premium. The underwriter assesses the applicant's risk characteristics, classifies the risk (preferred, standard, substandard, or declined), and determines the appropriate premium. Underwriting tools include applications, medical exams (for life and health), inspection reports, credit reports, motor vehicle records, and loss history (CLUE reports for property insurance). The goal is to achieve a balanced book of business with an acceptable loss ratio.

Claims handling involves: receiving notice of loss, investigating the claim (determining coverage, cause, and extent of loss), evaluating the claim (applying policy provisions, deductibles, and limits), and settling the claim (paying the appropriate amount). The claims adjuster may be a company adjuster (employee of the insurer), independent adjuster (contracted by the insurer), or public adjuster (hired by the insured to negotiate with the insurer). Unfair claims practices include unreasonable delays, lowball offers, failure to investigate, and misrepresenting policy provisions.

Insurance is regulated primarily at the state level under the authority affirmed by the McCarran-Ferguson Act (1945). Each state has an insurance department responsible for licensing insurers and producers, approving policy forms and rates, conducting market conduct examinations, handling consumer complaints, and overseeing insurer solvency. The NAIC develops model laws and regulations that states may adopt to promote uniformity. State guaranty associations protect policyholders if an insurer becomes insolvent, paying claims up to specified limits (typically $300,000-$500,000).

Producer licensing requires completing pre-licensing education, passing the state licensing exam, submitting a license application, and undergoing a background check. Licenses must be renewed periodically (typically every 2 years) with continuing education requirements (typically 24 hours per renewal period, including ethics). Producers have a duty to act in the best interest of their clients, disclose material information, avoid misrepresentation, and comply with all applicable laws and regulations. Violations can result in fines, license suspension or revocation, and criminal prosecution.

Insurance companies operate through several key functions: underwriting (evaluating and selecting risks to insure, setting premiums, and determining policy terms), claims management (investigating, evaluating, and settling claims), actuarial science (using statistical methods to calculate premiums, reserves, and financial projections), marketing and distribution (selling policies through agents, brokers, and direct channels), and investment management (investing premium income to generate returns that support claims payments and profitability).

The underwriting process evaluates risk factors to determine insurability and appropriate premium. For life insurance: age, health history, family medical history, occupation, hobbies, tobacco use, and driving record. For property insurance: construction type, location, fire protection class, occupancy, and loss history. For auto insurance: driver age, driving record, vehicle type, annual mileage, and geographic location. Underwriters use classification systems to group similar risks and apply appropriate rates.

Insurance company financial strength is evaluated by rating agencies: A.M. Best (the primary insurance rating agency), Standard & Poor's, Moody's, and Fitch. These ratings assess the insurer's ability to meet its ongoing obligations to policyholders. A.M. Best ratings range from A++ (Superior) to F (In Liquidation). Agents and brokers have a responsibility to place coverage with financially sound insurers. State guaranty funds provide limited protection if an insurer becomes insolvent, but coverage limits vary by state and are typically much lower than policy limits.

Insurance is primarily regulated at the state level. The McCarran-Ferguson Act of 1945 affirmed that states have the primary authority to regulate the insurance industry, with federal law applying only to the extent that state law does not adequately regulate the business of insurance. Each state has a Department of Insurance (or equivalent) headed by an Insurance Commissioner (elected or appointed) who oversees insurer licensing, rate approval, market conduct examinations, consumer complaints, and insurer solvency.

Rate regulation varies by state: prior approval (rates must be approved before use), file-and-use (rates are filed and can be used immediately, subject to later disapproval), use-and-file (rates can be used immediately and filed within a specified period), and open competition (no filing required, market forces determine rates). The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) is a voluntary organization of state insurance commissioners that develops model laws and regulations, promotes uniformity across states, and provides regulatory support services.

Producer (agent/broker) licensing requires passing a state examination, completing pre-licensing education, submitting a license application, and passing a background check. Continuing education is required for license renewal (typically 24 hours every 2 years, including ethics). Producers must maintain separate trust accounts for premium funds (fiduciary responsibility). Producers can be disciplined for: misrepresentation, twisting (inducing a policyholder to replace existing coverage through misrepresentation), rebating (returning part of the premium as an inducement to purchase), and unfair claims practices.

The claims process begins when the insured reports a loss to the insurer or agent. The insured's duties after a loss include: providing prompt notice (as soon as practicable), protecting the property from further damage, cooperating with the insurer's investigation, providing a sworn proof of loss (within 60 days for most policies), and submitting to examination under oath if requested. The insurer assigns a claims adjuster who investigates the claim, determines coverage, evaluates the loss, and negotiates a settlement.

Types of claims adjusters: company/staff adjusters (employees of the insurance company), independent adjusters (contracted by insurers to handle claims, especially in areas where the insurer has no local staff or during catastrophes), public adjusters (hired by the insured to represent their interests in the claims process — paid a percentage of the settlement, typically 10-15%), and adjusters from TPA (third-party administrators who handle claims for self-insured entities). The adjuster must act in good faith and deal fairly with the insured. Bad faith claims handling can result in extra-contractual damages (damages beyond the policy limits).

Unfair claims settlement practices (prohibited by state law) include: misrepresenting policy provisions, failing to acknowledge claims promptly, failing to adopt reasonable standards for investigation, refusing to pay claims without conducting a reasonable investigation, not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt and fair settlements when liability is clear, compelling insureds to institute litigation by offering substantially less than amounts ultimately recovered, and making claim payments without indicating the coverage under which payment is made.

Insurance is distributed through multiple channels. Independent agents (brokers) represent multiple insurance companies and can shop the market for the best coverage and price for their clients. They own their book of business (expirations) and can move clients between carriers. Captive (exclusive) agents represent a single insurance company and sell only that company's products. The company typically owns the book of business. Direct writers sell directly to consumers without agents (online, phone, mail). Managing General Agents (MGAs) are specialized intermediaries with underwriting authority from insurers — they can bind coverage, issue policies, and sometimes handle claims on behalf of the insurer.

The choice of distribution channel affects the consumer: independent agents provide choice and advocacy, captive agents provide deep knowledge of one company's products, and direct writers often provide lower premiums (no agent commission). Surplus lines brokers are a specialized distribution channel for non-admitted insurance — they must hold a specific surplus lines license and have expertise in placing hard-to-insure risks. Wholesale brokers act as intermediaries between retail agents/brokers and insurers, providing access to markets that the retail agent cannot access directly.

Deductible: the amount the insured must pay before the insurer pays. Types include: flat dollar (fixed amount per claim), percentage (percentage of the insured value, common in hurricane and earthquake coverage), aggregate (total deductible for all claims in a policy period), and elimination period (waiting period for disability and LTC insurance). Higher deductibles reduce premiums because the insured retains more risk. The optimal deductible balances premium savings against the insured's ability to absorb losses.

Coinsurance: in property insurance, a provision requiring the insured to maintain coverage equal to a specified percentage of value (typically 80%). In health insurance, the percentage of covered expenses shared between the insurer and the insured after the deductible is met (e.g., 80/20 coinsurance means the insurer pays 80% and the insured pays 20% until the out-of-pocket maximum is reached). Policy limits: per-occurrence limit (maximum for any single claim), aggregate limit (maximum for all claims in a policy period), and split limits (separate limits for different types of coverage, common in auto liability).

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) is a voluntary organization of state insurance regulators that develops model laws, regulations, and guidelines. While NAIC model laws are not binding, most states adopt them in whole or in part, creating substantial uniformity in insurance regulation across states. Key NAIC initiatives include: the Insurance Regulatory Information System (IRIS) for monitoring insurer financial health, the System for Electronic Rate and Form Filing (SERFF) for streamlining rate and form approvals, and the Uniform Certificate of Authority Application (UCAA) for standardizing insurer licensing across states.
