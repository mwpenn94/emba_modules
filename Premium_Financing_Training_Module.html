How to Use This Manual

Premium financing is a specialized financial arrangement in which a third-party lender provides funds to pay insurance premiums on behalf of the policyholder. The policyholder repays the loan with interest over time. Premium financing is used in two primary contexts: life insurance premium financing (for high-net-worth individuals purchasing large permanent life insurance policies) and commercial premium financing (for businesses spreading the cost of property, casualty, and other commercial insurance premiums over installments rather than paying the full annual premium upfront).

This manual covers both contexts, with emphasis on life insurance premium financing for high-net-worth (HNW) planning. Life insurance premium financing is a sophisticated strategy that requires careful analysis of interest rate risk, policy performance risk, collateral requirements, and exit strategy planning. It is appropriate only for clients with substantial assets and sophisticated financial understanding.

Premium financing involves three parties: the policyholder (borrower), the insurance company (issuing the policy), and the premium finance company or bank (lender). The lender pays the insurance premium directly to the insurer. The policyholder signs a premium finance agreement specifying the loan amount, interest rate, repayment terms, and collateral. The insurance policy itself serves as primary collateral (the lender is named as a collateral assignee), with additional collateral (cash, securities, letters of credit) often required.

The economic rationale for premium financing depends on the context. For commercial premium financing, the rationale is cash flow management — businesses can preserve working capital by spreading premium payments over the policy term rather than making a large lump-sum payment. For life insurance premium financing, the rationale is leverage — the policyholder borrows at a relatively low interest rate to fund a policy whose internal rate of return (death benefit relative to premiums) exceeds the borrowing cost. This is particularly attractive when the policyholder has substantial assets but prefers not to liquidate them to pay premiums.

Life insurance premium financing is typically used by high-net-worth individuals purchasing large permanent life insurance policies ($5 million to $100 million+ in death benefit) for estate planning, wealth transfer, or business succession purposes. The policyholder borrows the premium payments from a bank or premium finance company, using the policy's cash value as primary collateral and additional assets (typically a letter of credit or marketable securities) as supplemental collateral. The goal is to obtain the insurance coverage without liquidating existing investments or disrupting the client's overall financial plan.

Key risks of life insurance premium financing include: interest rate risk (if the loan rate increases, the cost of financing may exceed the policy's internal rate of return), policy performance risk (if the policy's cash value grows more slowly than projected, additional collateral may be required), collateral call risk (if collateral values decline, the lender may demand additional collateral or loan repayment), and exit strategy risk (the policyholder must have a viable plan to repay the loan, whether through policy cash value, other assets, or death benefit). A well-structured premium financing arrangement includes stress testing under adverse scenarios.

The exit strategy is critical. Common exit strategies include: (1) using the policy's accumulated cash value to repay the loan after a specified period (typically 7-15 years), (2) repaying the loan from other assets at a planned future date, (3) repaying from the death benefit (if the insured dies while the loan is outstanding), or (4) surrendering the policy and using the cash value to repay the loan (least desirable, as it eliminates the insurance coverage). The financing arrangement should be reviewed annually to assess whether the strategy remains viable given current interest rates, policy performance, and the client's financial situation.

Commercial premium financing allows businesses to spread the cost of insurance premiums over installments (typically 9-10 monthly payments) rather than paying the full annual premium upfront. This preserves working capital for business operations and investment. The premium finance company pays the full premium to the insurer and the business repays the loan with interest. The interest rate is typically based on the prime rate plus a margin. The insurance policy serves as collateral — if the business defaults on the loan, the premium finance company can cancel the policy and receive the unearned premium refund.

Commercial premium financing is widely used for property and casualty insurance, workers' compensation, commercial auto, general liability, and professional liability policies. The process is straightforward: the insurance agent or broker arranges the financing at the time of policy placement, the business signs a premium finance agreement, and the premium finance company pays the insurer. Monthly payments include principal and interest. The total cost of financing (interest charges) is typically modest relative to the benefit of preserving cash flow.

Premium financing is regulated at the state level. Most states have premium finance laws that specify: maximum interest rates, required disclosures to the borrower, cancellation procedures (including notice requirements), and licensing requirements for premium finance companies. The premium finance agreement must disclose the total premium amount, the down payment, the amount financed, the finance charge, the annual percentage rate (APR), the number and amount of installments, and the cancellation provisions.

For life insurance premium financing, additional regulatory considerations include: insurable interest requirements (the policyholder must have a legitimate insurable interest, not just a speculative interest in the death benefit), stranger-originated life insurance (STOLI) prohibitions (arrangements where an investor with no insurable interest initiates a policy on another person's life are prohibited in most states), and suitability requirements (the financing arrangement must be suitable for the client's financial situation and objectives). Advisers recommending premium financing must conduct thorough due diligence and document the suitability analysis.

Premium financing is a lending arrangement where a third-party lender provides funds to pay insurance premiums. The insured makes a down payment (typically 20-30%) and the lender pays the balance directly to the insurer. The insured repays in monthly installments plus interest. The lender takes a security interest in the policy (unearned premium and cash value) as collateral. In Arizona, premium finance companies must be licensed by DIFI.

The agreement must disclose: total premium, down payment, amount financed, finance charge, APR, installment amounts, and cancellation provisions. On default, the lender can cancel the policy (with 10 days' notice to the insured) and receive the unearned premium refund. If the refund exceeds the loan balance, excess is returned to the insured; if less, the lender can pursue the deficiency.

Arizona regulates premium financing under ARS Title 20, Chapter 6, Article 14. Premium finance companies must obtain a license from DIFI before conducting business. License requirements include: filing an application with DIFI, maintaining minimum net worth requirements, posting a surety bond, and designating a registered agent in Arizona. The license must be renewed annually. DIFI has the authority to examine the books and records of licensed premium finance companies and to take enforcement action for violations.

The premium finance agreement must contain specific disclosures required by Arizona law: the total premium amount, the amount of the down payment, the principal balance (amount financed), the finance charge in dollars, the annual percentage rate (APR), the number, amount, and due dates of installments, a description of the insurance policies being financed, the cancellation provisions and the insured's rights upon cancellation, and a statement that the premium finance company will have a security interest in the policy. The agreement must be signed by the insured (or their authorized representative) before the premium finance company advances funds to the insurer.

Consider a 55-year-old business owner with a $30 million estate who needs $10 million of permanent life insurance for estate liquidity. Annual premium: $200,000 for a guaranteed universal life policy. Without premium financing, the client pays $200,000/year from cash flow or investments. With premium financing: the client makes a 25% down payment ($50,000) and finances the remaining $150,000. At a 5% interest rate, the annual interest cost is approximately $7,500. The client's investment portfolio earns 8% annually, so the $150,000 kept invested earns approximately $12,000 — a positive arbitrage of $4,500.

The key risks in this scenario: if interest rates rise to 8%, the arbitrage disappears and the client is paying more in interest than they would earn by investing the $150,000. If the client's investment portfolio declines, the lender may require additional collateral. If the policy underperforms (lower cash value than projected), the exit strategy may be delayed. The adviser must stress-test the arrangement under adverse scenarios (rising rates, poor investment returns, declining policy performance) and ensure the client understands and can absorb the downside risks.

Effective risk mitigation in premium financing requires addressing multiple risk factors. Interest rate risk can be mitigated through: fixed-rate loans (locking in the borrowing cost), interest rate caps (limiting the maximum rate), and shorter loan terms (reducing exposure to rate fluctuations). Collateral risk is managed through: conservative loan-to-value ratios (typically 70-80% of policy cash value), diversified collateral (not relying solely on the policy), and regular collateral monitoring with predetermined trigger points for additional collateral calls.

Policy performance risk (the risk that the policy's cash value does not grow as projected) is mitigated through: conservative illustration assumptions (using current rates rather than maximum non-guaranteed rates), guaranteed universal life policies (which provide guaranteed death benefits regardless of market performance), and regular policy reviews with the ability to adjust the financing structure. The adviser should present stress-test scenarios showing the impact of: interest rates 200-300 basis points above current levels, policy returns at the guaranteed minimum, and simultaneous adverse movements in both variables.

Premium financing regulations are designed to protect consumers from predatory lending practices. Key consumer protections include: the right to cancel the premium finance agreement within a specified period (typically 10 days) without penalty, the right to receive clear and conspicuous disclosure of all terms and costs before signing, the right to receive notice before the premium finance company cancels the underlying insurance policy (typically 10 days' notice to the insured and the insurer), and the right to receive any unearned premium refund that exceeds the outstanding loan balance. The premium finance company cannot charge a prepayment penalty if the insured pays off the loan early.

Arizona law requires premium finance companies to file their rates and forms with DIFI. The maximum service charge is regulated and varies based on the amount financed and the term of the agreement. Premium finance companies are subject to examination by DIFI, which reviews: compliance with disclosure requirements, accuracy of APR calculations, proper handling of cancellation and refund procedures, adequacy of record-keeping, and treatment of consumer complaints. Violations can result in administrative penalties, license revocation, and civil liability to affected consumers.

The key to successful premium financing is understanding the economics: the cost of borrowing must be justified by either the cash flow benefit (commercial) or the arbitrage opportunity (HNW life insurance). Advisers must conduct thorough suitability analysis, stress-test the arrangement under adverse scenarios, and ensure the client fully understands the risks including interest rate risk, collateral risk, and policy performance risk.
