How to Use This Manual

The Property and Casualty (P&C) insurance examination qualifies individuals to sell property insurance (protecting physical assets against loss or damage) and casualty insurance (protecting against liability for injuries or damage caused to others). P&C insurance is fundamentally different from life and health insurance: P&C policies are contracts of indemnity (the insured is compensated for actual losses, not a predetermined benefit amount), they are typically short-term (annual policies), and they cover both first-party losses (damage to the insured's own property) and third-party losses (liability claims from others).

This manual covers six chapters: property insurance principles, homeowners insurance, auto insurance, commercial insurance, casualty and liability, and regulation. The exam typically includes approximately 150 questions. Focus on understanding the differences between policy types, coverage limits, exclusions, and the conditions under which claims are paid or denied.

The principle of indemnity limits insurance recovery to the actual financial loss suffered, preventing the insured from profiting from a loss. Actual cash value (ACV) equals replacement cost minus depreciation. Replacement cost value (RCV) covers the cost to replace damaged property with new property of like kind and quality, without deduction for depreciation. Most homeowners prefer replacement cost coverage because ACV can result in significantly lower claim payments for older items.

Coinsurance in property insurance requires the insured to maintain coverage equal to a specified percentage (typically 80%) of the property's replacement cost. If the insured fails to meet the coinsurance requirement, the insurer will only pay a proportional share of the loss. The coinsurance formula: Payment = (Amount of Insurance / Required Amount) x Loss - Deductible. For example, if a building worth $500,000 is insured for $300,000 with an 80% coinsurance clause, the required amount is $400,000. For a $100,000 loss: Payment = ($300,000 / $400,000) x $100,000 = $75,000 minus the deductible.

Subrogation allows the insurer, after paying a claim, to pursue recovery from the third party responsible for the loss. For example, if another driver causes an accident and the insured's insurer pays for the damage, the insurer can seek reimbursement from the at-fault driver's insurer. The insured cannot waive the insurer's right of subrogation after a loss. Other insurance clauses determine how multiple policies covering the same loss share the payment: pro rata (each insurer pays proportionally based on policy limits), excess (one policy is primary, the other pays only amounts exceeding the primary's limits), or escape (one policy pays nothing if other insurance exists).

Homeowners insurance policies are standardized forms (HO-1 through HO-8) providing coverage for the dwelling, other structures, personal property, loss of use, and personal liability. HO-3 (Special Form) is the most common policy: it covers the dwelling on an open-perils basis (all risks except those specifically excluded) and personal property on a named-perils basis (only the 16 named perils). HO-5 (Comprehensive Form) covers both the dwelling and personal property on an open-perils basis, providing the broadest coverage. HO-4 (Contents Broad Form) is for renters, covering personal property only. HO-6 (Unit-Owners Form) is for condominium owners.

Standard homeowners exclusions include flood, earthquake, war, nuclear hazard, ordinance or law, neglect, intentional loss, and earth movement. Flood insurance is available through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). Earthquake coverage is available as an endorsement or separate policy. Personal liability coverage (Coverage E, typically $100,000-$300,000) protects against claims for bodily injury or property damage caused by the insured. Medical payments to others (Coverage F, typically $1,000-$5,000) pays medical expenses for guests injured on the insured's property regardless of fault.

The Personal Auto Policy (PAP) provides four coverages: Part A (Liability) covers bodily injury and property damage the insured causes to others; Part B (Medical Payments) covers medical expenses for the insured and passengers regardless of fault; Part C (Uninsured/Underinsured Motorists) covers injuries caused by drivers with no or insufficient insurance; and Part D (Physical Damage) covers damage to the insured's own vehicle through collision (any impact) and other-than-collision (comprehensive: theft, fire, vandalism, weather, animal strikes).

Liability limits are expressed as split limits (e.g., 100/300/50: $100,000 per person bodily injury, $300,000 per accident bodily injury, $50,000 per accident property damage) or combined single limit (one amount covering all liability per accident). Most states require minimum liability coverage. No-fault auto insurance (Personal Injury Protection, PIP) requires each driver's own insurer to pay their medical expenses regardless of fault, up to specified limits. In no-fault states, the right to sue for non-economic damages (pain and suffering) is restricted unless injuries meet a specified threshold (verbal or monetary).

Commercial property insurance covers business-owned buildings, equipment, inventory, and other business personal property. The Business Owners Policy (BOP) packages property and liability coverage for small to medium businesses at a lower cost than purchasing separate policies. Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance covers third-party claims for bodily injury, property damage, personal injury (libel, slander), and advertising injury arising from business operations. CGL policies are typically written on an occurrence basis (covering claims arising from incidents during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is filed) or claims-made basis (covering claims filed during the policy period, regardless of when the incident occurred).

Workers' compensation insurance covers employees for work-related injuries and illnesses, providing medical benefits, disability income, rehabilitation, and death benefits. Workers' comp is a no-fault system — benefits are paid regardless of who caused the injury. In exchange, employees generally cannot sue their employer for work-related injuries (the exclusive remedy doctrine). Employers' liability insurance (Part B of the workers' comp policy) covers the employer against lawsuits that fall outside the workers' comp system. Professional liability (errors and omissions, or E&O) insurance covers professionals against claims of negligence, errors, or omissions in their professional services.

Umbrella liability insurance provides excess liability coverage above the limits of underlying policies (homeowners, auto, commercial). Umbrella policies typically provide $1-$10 million in additional coverage and may also cover claims excluded by underlying policies (drop-down coverage). The insured must maintain specified minimum limits on underlying policies to qualify for umbrella coverage. Umbrella policies are relatively inexpensive because they only pay after underlying limits are exhausted.

Inland marine insurance covers property in transit and specialized equipment (contractors' equipment, fine arts, jewelry, musical instruments). Ocean marine insurance covers ships, cargo, and related liabilities. Surety bonds guarantee performance of a contractual obligation (unlike insurance, which transfers risk, a surety bond involves three parties: the principal who must perform, the obligee who is protected, and the surety who guarantees performance). Fidelity bonds protect employers against employee dishonesty (theft, embezzlement, fraud).

Property and casualty insurance is regulated at the state level. State insurance departments oversee licensing, rate approval, market conduct, and consumer protection. Rate regulation approaches include prior approval (rates must be approved before use), file-and-use (rates are filed and can be used immediately), use-and-file (rates can be used and must be filed within a specified period), and open competition (no filing required). The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) develops model laws and regulations that states may adopt, promoting uniformity across state lines.

Insurance producers must be licensed in each state where they sell insurance. Continuing education is required for license renewal. Unfair claims settlement practices include failing to acknowledge claims promptly, failing to investigate claims thoroughly, making lowball settlement offers, and failing to provide a reasonable explanation for claim denials. The insured has a duty to cooperate with the insurer's investigation, provide proof of loss, and mitigate further damage. Failure to comply with policy conditions can result in denial of the claim.

Homeowners insurance policies come in several standardized forms. HO-1 (Basic Form) covers 10 named perils only and is rarely used. HO-2 (Broad Form) covers 16 named perils, adding perils such as falling objects, weight of ice/snow, and accidental discharge of water. HO-3 (Special Form) is the most common homeowners policy: it provides open-peril (all-risk) coverage on the dwelling and other structures, and named-peril coverage on personal property. Open-peril means everything is covered unless specifically excluded.

HO-4 (Contents Broad Form) is for renters, covering personal property on a named-peril basis with liability coverage but no dwelling coverage. HO-5 (Comprehensive Form) provides open-peril coverage on both the dwelling AND personal property, the broadest coverage available. HO-6 (Unit-Owners Form) is for condominium owners. HO-8 (Modified Coverage Form) is for older homes where replacement cost exceeds market value, covering the dwelling on a functional replacement cost basis.

Replacement cost coverage pays the cost to repair or replace damaged property with materials of like kind and quality, without deduction for depreciation. Actual cash value (ACV) coverage pays the replacement cost minus depreciation. The coinsurance clause requires the insured to maintain coverage equal to a specified percentage (typically 80%) of the property's replacement cost. If underinsured, the insurer pays only a proportional share: Payment = (Amount Carried / Amount Required) x Loss - Deductible.

Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance covers businesses against third-party claims for bodily injury, property damage, personal injury, and advertising injury. Coverage A covers bodily injury and property damage from operations and products. Coverage B covers personal and advertising injury (libel, slander, false arrest). Coverage C covers medical payments regardless of fault. CGL policies can be occurrence-based (covers incidents during the policy period regardless of when claimed) or claims-made (covers claims both made and reported during the policy period).

Umbrella liability insurance provides excess coverage above underlying policy limits and may provide broader coverage for claims not covered by underlying policies (subject to a self-insured retention). Professional liability (E&O) insurance covers claims arising from professional services, typically on a claims-made basis. Directors and Officers (D&O) liability insurance protects corporate directors and officers against claims alleging wrongful acts in their management capacity.

Personal auto insurance consists of several coverage parts. Part A (Liability Coverage) pays for bodily injury and property damage the insured causes to others. Split limits are expressed as three numbers (e.g., 100/300/100): $100,000 per person bodily injury, $300,000 per accident bodily injury, $100,000 per accident property damage. Combined single limit (CSL) provides one limit for all liability claims from an accident. Most states require minimum liability coverage.

Part B (Medical Payments/PIP) pays medical expenses for the insured and passengers regardless of fault. Personal Injury Protection (PIP) in no-fault states provides broader coverage including lost wages and essential services. Part C (Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist) pays when the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage. Part D (Physical Damage) includes collision coverage (damage from collision with another object, regardless of fault, minus deductible) and comprehensive coverage (other-than-collision: theft, vandalism, weather, animal strikes, glass breakage).

Auto insurance rating factors include: driver age and experience (younger drivers pay more), driving record (accidents and violations increase premiums), vehicle type (sports cars cost more than sedans), annual mileage, geographic location (urban areas have higher rates), credit score (in most states), and coverage selections. Discounts are available for: multi-policy bundling, good student, defensive driving course, anti-theft devices, low mileage, and multi-car policies.

The risk management process consists of five steps: (1) Identify risks — determine all potential sources of loss (property, liability, personnel, net income). (2) Analyze risks — evaluate the frequency (how often losses occur) and severity (how large losses are) of each identified risk. (3) Select risk management techniques — choose the appropriate method for handling each risk. (4) Implement the chosen techniques. (5) Monitor and review — continuously evaluate the effectiveness of the risk management program and adjust as needed.

Risk management techniques include: avoidance (eliminating the risk entirely — e.g., not owning a swimming pool), loss control/reduction (reducing the frequency or severity of losses — e.g., installing sprinklers, security systems), retention (self-insuring through deductibles, self-insured retentions, or captive insurance companies), and transfer (shifting the risk to another party through insurance, hold-harmless agreements, or contractual risk transfer). The general rule: retain high-frequency/low-severity risks (deductibles) and transfer low-frequency/high-severity risks (insurance).

Workers' compensation insurance provides benefits to employees who suffer work-related injuries or illnesses, regardless of fault. Benefits include: medical expenses (all reasonable and necessary treatment), disability income (temporary total, temporary partial, permanent total, permanent partial — typically 66 2/3% of average weekly wage), rehabilitation (vocational and physical), and death benefits (funeral expenses and income replacement for dependents). Workers' compensation is a statutory coverage — employers are required by law to provide it in all states except Texas (where it's optional).

Workers' compensation operates on an exclusive remedy basis: the employee receives guaranteed benefits regardless of fault, but in exchange gives up the right to sue the employer for negligence (with limited exceptions for intentional harm). Classification codes determine the premium rate based on the type of work performed — clerical workers have much lower rates than construction workers. Experience modification factors adjust premiums based on the employer's actual loss history compared to the industry average: a mod factor below 1.0 indicates better-than-average experience (premium credit), above 1.0 indicates worse (premium surcharge).

The Business Owners Policy (BOP) is a package policy designed for small to medium-sized businesses that combines property coverage, business income coverage, and liability coverage in a single policy at a lower premium than purchasing each coverage separately. BOPs are available for eligible businesses (typically retail stores, offices, apartments, and similar low-to-moderate hazard operations). BOPs are NOT available for auto dealers, bars, banks, or contractors.

BOP property coverage is written on a special form (open-peril) basis for the building and business personal property. Business income coverage (also called business interruption) pays for lost income and continuing expenses when a covered peril forces the business to suspend operations. Extra expense coverage pays for additional costs to continue operations during the restoration period (e.g., renting temporary space). BOP liability coverage is equivalent to the CGL occurrence form, covering bodily injury, property damage, personal injury, and advertising injury.

Inland marine insurance covers property in transit, property held by bailees, mobile equipment, and instrumentalities of transportation and communication. Unlike standard property insurance (which covers property at a fixed location), inland marine covers property that moves or is used in multiple locations. Common inland marine coverages include: builders risk (covers buildings under construction), installation floaters (covers equipment being installed), transit policies (covers goods in transit), bailee coverage (covers property of others in the insured's care, custody, or control), and equipment floaters (covers mobile equipment like contractors' tools and medical equipment).

Inland marine policies are typically written on an open-peril (all-risk) basis with few exclusions, providing broader coverage than standard property forms. Valuation is usually on a replacement cost or agreed value basis. Inland marine is one of the most profitable lines of insurance because losses are generally well-managed and the coverage is tailored to specific risks. The surplus lines market often provides inland marine coverage for unusual or high-value risks that the admitted market cannot accommodate, such as fine art, jewelry collections, and specialized industrial equipment.

Flood insurance is excluded from standard property policies and must be purchased separately. The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) provides flood coverage for residential and commercial properties in participating communities. NFIP limits: $250,000 for residential buildings, $100,000 for residential contents, $500,000 for commercial buildings, $500,000 for commercial contents. Private flood insurance is available for higher limits and broader coverage. Earthquake insurance is also excluded from standard policies and must be purchased as a separate policy or endorsement. Earthquake policies typically have high percentage deductibles (10-20% of the coverage amount) rather than flat dollar deductibles.

Actual Cash Value (ACV) calculation: Replacement Cost - Depreciation = ACV. A 10-year-old roof with a 20-year life and $20,000 replacement cost has an ACV of $10,000 ($20,000 - 50% depreciation). Under replacement cost coverage, the insurer pays the full $20,000 to replace the roof (no depreciation deduction). Most policies pay ACV initially and the difference between ACV and replacement cost after the repairs are completed.

Other insurance (coordination of benefits) clauses determine how multiple policies share a loss. The most common method is pro rata by limits: each insurer pays its proportion of the total limits. For example, if Policy A has $100,000 limits and Policy B has $200,000 limits, and the loss is $150,000: Policy A pays 1/3 ($50,000) and Policy B pays 2/3 ($100,000). The excess/primary method designates one policy as primary (pays first up to its limits) and the other as excess (pays only after the primary is exhausted). Auto insurance typically uses the policy following the vehicle as primary.

Subrogation allows the insurer to recover claim payments from the responsible third party. Example: the insured's car is damaged by another driver. The insured's collision coverage pays for repairs (minus deductible). The insurer then subrogate against the at-fault driver's liability insurance to recover the claim payment. If successful, the insured's deductible is also recovered. The insured must cooperate with subrogation efforts and must not do anything to impair the insurer's subrogation rights (such as signing a release with the at-fault party without the insurer's consent).

13. Which homeowners form provides open-peril coverage on BOTH the dwelling and personal property? A) HO-2 B) HO-3 C) HO-5 D) HO-8. Answer: C. Workers' comp is an exclusive remedy system: the employee receives guaranteed benefits regardless of fault but gives up the right to sue the employer for negligence.

15. The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) maximum coverage for a residential building is: A) $100,000 B) $250,000 C) $500,000 D) $1,000,000. Answer: B. NFIP limits: $250,000 residential building, $100,000 residential contents, $500,000 commercial building, $500,000 commercial contents.

16. An experience modification factor of 0.85 means: A) The employer's losses are 85% worse than average B) The employer receives a 15% premium credit C) The employer pays 85% more than manual rates D) The employer's losses are 15% worse than average. Answer: B. A mod factor below 1.0 indicates better-than-average loss experience. 0.85 means the employer pays 85% of the manual rate — a 15% credit.

The coinsurance penalty formula in property insurance: if the insured carries less than the required percentage of coverage, the insurer pays: (Amount Carried / Amount Required) x Loss - Deductible. Example: building worth $500,000 with 80% coinsurance requirement. Required coverage: $400,000. Insured carries only $300,000. Loss: $200,000. Payment: ($300,000 / $400,000) x $200,000 = $150,000. The insured bears the $50,000 shortfall as a penalty for underinsuring. The coinsurance clause incentivizes adequate coverage and prevents insureds from purchasing minimal coverage on high-value properties.

Umbrella liability insurance provides excess coverage above underlying policies (auto, homeowners, CGL) and may also provide broader coverage for claims not covered by underlying policies (drop-down coverage). Personal umbrella policies typically require minimum underlying limits ($250,000/$500,000 auto liability, $300,000 homeowners liability) and provide $1-10 million in additional coverage at relatively low premiums. Commercial umbrella policies work similarly for businesses, sitting above the CGL, auto liability, and employers liability policies.

The property and casualty insurance industry is cyclical, alternating between hard markets (rising premiums, stricter underwriting, reduced capacity) and soft markets (declining premiums, relaxed underwriting, abundant capacity). Understanding the insurance cycle is important for agents advising clients on coverage timing and renewal strategies. In hard markets, agents should help clients improve their risk profiles to obtain better terms, explore alternative risk transfer mechanisms, and consider higher deductibles to manage premium increases.

Property and casualty insurance is the backbone of risk management for individuals and businesses alike. Mastering the fundamentals covered in this manual — policy structure, coverage analysis, claims handling, and regulatory compliance — provides the foundation for a successful career in this essential industry.

Property and casualty professionals serve as trusted advisors helping clients navigate the complex landscape of risk, ensuring adequate protection for their most valuable assets and liabilities.

NFIP limits: $250,000 for residential buildings, $100,000 for residential contents, $500,000 for commercial buildings, $500,000 for commercial contents. Private flood insurance is available for higher limits and broader coverage. Earthquake insurance is also excluded from standard policies and must be purchased as a separate policy or endorsement. Earthquake policies typically have high percentage deductibles (10-20% of the coverage amount) rather than flat dollar deductibles.

Insurance Claim Scenarios and Calculations

Actual Cash Value (ACV) calculation: Replacement Cost - Depreciation = ACV. A 10-year-old roof with a 20-year life and $20,000 replacement cost has an ACV of $10,000 ($20,000 - 50% depreciation). Under replacement cost coverage, the insurer pays the full $20,000 to replace the roof (no depreciation deduction). Most policies pay ACV initially and the difference between ACV and replacement cost after the repairs are completed.

Other insurance (coordination of benefits) clauses determine how multiple policies share a loss. The most common method is pro rata by limits: each insurer pays its proportion of the total limits. For example, if Policy A has $100,000 limits and Policy B has $200,000 limits, and the loss is $150,000: Policy A pays 1/3 ($50,000) and Policy B pays 2/3 ($100,000). The excess/primary method designates one policy as primary (pays first up to its limits) and the other as excess (pays only after the primary is exhausted). Auto insurance typically uses the policy following the vehicle as primary.

Subrogation allows the insurer to recover claim payments from the responsible third party. Example: the insured's car is damaged by another driver. The insured's collision coverage pays for repairs (minus deductible). The insurer then subrogate against the at-fault driver's liability insurance to recover the claim payment. If successful, the insured's deductible is also recovered. The insured must cooperate with subrogation efforts and must not do anything to impair the insurer's subrogation rights (such as signing a release with the at-fault party without the insurer's consent).

P&C Additional Practice Questions

13. Which homeowners form provides open-peril coverage on BOTH the dwelling and personal property? A) HO-2 B) HO-3 C) HO-5 D) HO-8. Answer: C. HO-5 (Comprehensive Form) provides open-peril coverage on both dwelling and personal property. HO-3 provides open-peril on the dwelling but named-peril on personal property.

14. Workers' compensation operates on what legal principle? A) Negligence B) Strict liability C) Exclusive remedy D) Comparative fault. Answer: C. Workers' comp is an exclusive remedy system: the employee receives guaranteed benefits regardless of fault but gives up the right to sue the employer for negligence.

15. The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) maximum coverage for a residential building is: A) $100,000 B) $250,000 C) $500,000 D) $1,000,000. Answer: B. NFIP limits: $250,000 residential building, $100,000 residential contents, $500,000 commercial building, $500,000 commercial contents.

16. An experience modification factor of 0.85 means: A) The employer's losses are 85% worse than average B) The employer receives a 15% premium credit C) The employer pays 85% more than manual rates D) The employer's losses are 15% worse than average. Answer: B. A mod factor below 1.0 indicates better-than-average loss experience. 0.85 means the employer pays 85% of the manual rate — a 15% credit.

P&C Final Key Facts

The coinsurance penalty formula in property insurance: if the insured carries less than the required percentage of coverage, the insurer pays: (Amount Carried / Amount Required) x Loss - Deductible. Example: building worth $500,000 with 80% coinsurance requirement. Required coverage: $400,000. Insured carries only $300,000. Loss: $200,000. Payment: ($300,000 / $400,000) x $200,000 = $150,000. The insured bears the $50,000 shortfall as a penalty for underinsuring. The coinsurance clause incentivizes adequate coverage and prevents insureds from purchasing minimal coverage on high-value properties.

Umbrella liability insurance provides excess coverage above underlying policies (auto, homeowners, CGL) and may also provide broader coverage for claims not covered by underlying policies (drop-down coverage). Personal umbrella policies typically require minimum underlying limits ($250,000/$500,000 auto liability, $300,000 homeowners liability) and provide $1-10 million in additional coverage at relatively low premiums. Commercial umbrella policies work similarly for businesses, sitting above the CGL, auto liability, and employers liability policies.

The property and casualty insurance industry is cyclical, alternating between hard markets (rising premiums, stricter underwriting, reduced capacity) and soft markets (declining premiums, relaxed underwriting, abundant capacity). Understanding the insurance cycle is important for agents advising clients on coverage timing and renewal strategies. In hard markets, agents should help clients improve their risk profiles to obtain better terms, explore alternative risk transfer mechanisms, and consider higher deductibles to manage premium increases.

Property and casualty insurance is the backbone of risk management for individuals and businesses alike. Mastering the fundamentals covered in this manual — policy structure, coverage analysis, claims handling, and regulatory compliance — provides the foundation for a successful career in this essential industry.

Property and casualty professionals serve as trusted advisors helping clients navigate the complex landscape of risk, ensuring adequate protection for their most valuable assets and liabilities.
