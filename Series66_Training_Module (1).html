How to Use This Manual

The Series 66 (Uniform Combined State Law Examination) combines the content of the Series 63 (state securities law) and Series 65 (investment adviser law) into a single exam. Passing the Series 66 (along with the Series 7) qualifies an individual to act as both a securities agent and an investment adviser representative (IAR) at the state level. The Series 66 is required in most states for individuals who provide investment advice and also sell securities products.

The Series 66 tests four major areas: economic factors and business information (5%), investment vehicle characteristics (20%), client investment recommendations and strategies (30%), and laws, regulations, and guidelines (45%). The heaviest emphasis is on regulatory content — the Uniform Securities Act, state registration requirements, ethical practices, and fiduciary obligations. Unlike the Series 7, the Series 66 focuses on the advisory side of the business: understanding fiduciary duty, constructing suitable portfolios, and complying with state and federal regulations governing investment advisers.

Exam Overview

This section tests basic economic concepts relevant to investment advice. The business cycle (expansion, peak, contraction, trough) affects investment strategy. During expansion, equities generally outperform. During contraction, fixed income and defensive stocks tend to outperform. The yield curve reflects market expectations about future interest rates and economic conditions. A normal (upward-sloping) curve suggests expected growth. An inverted curve has historically preceded recessions.

Key economic indicators include GDP (the total value of goods and services produced), CPI (measures inflation), unemployment rate (lagging indicator), and the federal funds rate (the Fed's primary monetary policy tool). The Federal Reserve influences the economy through monetary policy: lowering rates stimulates growth (expansionary), raising rates slows growth (contractionary). Fiscal policy (government spending and taxation) is set by Congress. Understanding how these factors affect different asset classes is essential for making appropriate investment recommendations.

Financial statements provide essential information for evaluating investments. The balance sheet shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time. The income statement shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a period. The cash flow statement tracks cash inflows and outflows. Key ratios include: current ratio (current assets / current liabilities, measures liquidity), debt-to-equity ratio (total debt / total equity, measures leverage), P/E ratio (price / earnings per share, measures valuation), and return on equity (net income / shareholders' equity, measures profitability).

Common stock provides ownership, voting rights, and growth potential through capital appreciation and dividends. Preferred stock provides fixed income with priority over common stock. Bonds provide fixed income with return of principal at maturity. The inverse relationship between bond prices and interest rates is fundamental. Duration measures bond price sensitivity to rate changes. Credit ratings assess default risk. Municipal bonds offer tax-exempt income. Treasury securities offer the highest credit quality.

Options provide leverage and risk management. Calls give the right to buy; puts give the right to sell. Covered calls generate income; protective puts provide insurance. The Series 66 tests basic options knowledge — the Series 7 tests complex strategies and calculations. Understanding the risk-reward profile of each position is sufficient for the Series 66.

Mutual funds offer diversification, professional management, and liquidity. Open-end funds issue and redeem at NAV. Sales charges include front-end loads (Class A), back-end loads (Class B), and level loads (Class C). The expense ratio is the total annual cost. ETFs trade intraday with lower expenses and greater tax efficiency. Closed-end funds trade at premiums or discounts to NAV. Hedge funds are limited to accredited investors and use aggressive strategies (leverage, short selling, derivatives). Private equity invests in non-public companies. REITs invest in real estate and must distribute 90% of taxable income.

Variable annuities combine insurance features with investment subaccounts. Growth is tax-deferred. Withdrawals before 59 1/2 incur a 10% penalty. Surrender charges apply during the early years. The death benefit guarantees that beneficiaries receive at least the amount invested (minus withdrawals). Variable annuities are securities requiring both a securities license and an insurance license. Fixed annuities are NOT securities because the insurance company bears the investment risk.

Investment recommendations must be based on a thorough understanding of the client's financial situation, goals, and constraints. The investment policy statement (IPS) documents: return objectives (income, growth, or both), risk tolerance (conservative, moderate, aggressive), time horizon (short-term, intermediate, long-term), liquidity needs, tax situation, legal constraints, and unique circumstances. Risk tolerance is influenced by both the client's willingness to accept risk (psychological) and their ability to bear risk (financial capacity). The lower of the two should govern the recommendation.

Asset allocation is the primary determinant of portfolio returns (studies suggest it explains 90%+ of return variability). Strategic asset allocation establishes long-term target weights based on the client's risk-return profile. Tactical asset allocation allows short-term deviations to capitalize on market opportunities. Rebalancing returns the portfolio to target weights after market movements cause drift. Modern Portfolio Theory demonstrates that diversification with imperfectly correlated assets reduces portfolio risk without sacrificing expected return.

Retirement planning requires estimating future income needs, identifying income sources (Social Security, pensions, personal savings), and calculating the savings required to fill any gap. The 4% rule suggests withdrawing 4% of the portfolio in the first year of retirement, adjusted annually for inflation. Social Security benefits are based on the highest 35 years of earnings. Full Retirement Age is 67 for those born 1960+. Benefits can be claimed at 62 (reduced) or delayed to 70 (increased by 8% per year). Required Minimum Distributions from traditional retirement accounts begin at age 73.

Education planning vehicles include 529 plans (tax-free growth and withdrawals for qualified education expenses, $19,000 annual gift exclusion per beneficiary, 5-year gift tax averaging up to $95,000), Coverdell ESAs ($2,000 annual limit with income restrictions), and UTMA/UGMA custodial accounts (irrevocable gifts subject to kiddie tax). The AOTC provides up to $2,500 per student for the first four years of college. Coordination between 529 withdrawals and tax credits is essential to maximize the total benefit.

Tax-efficient investing considers the tax treatment of different account types and investment products. Tax-deferred accounts (traditional IRA, 401(k)) defer taxes until withdrawal. Tax-free accounts (Roth IRA, Roth 401(k)) provide tax-free growth and qualified withdrawals. Taxable accounts offer flexibility but generate current tax liability. Tax-loss harvesting involves selling securities at a loss to offset gains, subject to the wash sale rule (30-day window). Municipal bonds provide tax-exempt income, with tax-equivalent yield = muni yield / (1 - tax rate). Asset location strategy places tax-inefficient investments (bonds, REITs) in tax-advantaged accounts and tax-efficient investments (index funds, growth stocks) in taxable accounts.

The Uniform Securities Act (USA) is the model state securities law that provides the framework for state-level regulation of securities, broker-dealers, investment advisers, and their representatives. The USA requires registration of persons (broker-dealers, agents, investment advisers, investment adviser representatives) and securities in each state where they conduct business. The state securities administrator has broad authority to issue rules, conduct investigations, issue cease and desist orders, and deny, suspend, or revoke registrations.

Registration of broker-dealers and investment advisers is effective from the date of granting through December 31 of that year, with annual renewal required. An agent is any individual who represents a broker-dealer in effecting securities transactions. An investment adviser representative (IAR) is any individual who makes investment recommendations, manages client assets, or solicits for advisory services. Both agents and IARs must register in the states where they have a place of business or where they have retail clients.

The USA defines a security broadly to include stocks, bonds, notes, investment contracts, and interests in profit-sharing agreements. Exempt securities (those not required to register) include U.S. government securities, municipal bonds, bank securities, and securities listed on national exchanges. Exempt transactions include isolated non-issuer transactions, transactions with institutional investors, private placements, and fiduciary transactions. Even exempt securities and transactions are subject to the antifraud provisions of the USA.

Investment advisers are regulated at both the federal level (SEC, under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940) and the state level (under the USA). An investment adviser is any person who, for compensation, engages in the business of advising others about the value of securities or the advisability of investing in securities. The three-prong test requires: (1) providing advice about securities, (2) being in the business of providing such advice, and (3) receiving compensation for the advice. All three prongs must be met.

Federal registration (with the SEC) is required for advisers with $100 million or more in assets under management (AUM). State registration is required for advisers with less than $100 million AUM. Advisers with $100-110 million AUM may register with either the SEC or the state. The Form ADV is the registration document for investment advisers, consisting of Part 1 (firm information, filed with the SEC/state) and Part 2 (the brochure, delivered to clients). Part 2A describes the adviser's services, fees, investment strategies, and conflicts of interest. Part 2B (the brochure supplement) provides information about the specific individuals who provide advice to the client.

Investment advisers owe a fiduciary duty to their clients, requiring them to act in the client's best interest at all times. This includes the duty of loyalty (putting the client's interests first), the duty of care (providing advice with competence and diligence), and the duty to disclose all material conflicts of interest. The adviser must obtain the client's informed consent before engaging in transactions that present conflicts, such as agency cross transactions (where the adviser acts as broker for both buyer and seller) or principal transactions (where the adviser buys from or sells to the client from the adviser's own account).

The USA prohibits a wide range of unethical practices. For investment advisers and IARs, prohibited conduct includes: charging unreasonable fees, exercising discretion without written authorization, assigning an advisory contract without client consent, entering into performance-based fee arrangements with retail clients (with limited exceptions for qualified clients), borrowing from or lending to clients, failing to disclose conflicts of interest, and making unsuitable recommendations.

For broker-dealers and agents, prohibited conduct includes: churning (excessive trading), unauthorized trading, selling away (conducting transactions outside the firm), sharing in customer accounts (without firm approval and proportional contribution), guaranteeing against loss, making material misrepresentations or omissions, front-running, insider trading, and market manipulation. The antifraud provisions of the USA apply to ALL securities transactions, including exempt securities and exempt transactions. There is no exemption from the antifraud rules.

Investment advisers must register with either the SEC or the state, depending on assets under management (AUM). SEC registration is required for advisers with AUM of $100 million or more (the 'AUM threshold'). State registration is required for advisers with AUM below $100 million. The buffer zone ($100-$110 million) allows advisers to remain state-registered until AUM exceeds $110 million, and SEC-registered advisers need not withdraw until AUM falls below $90 million. This prevents frequent switching between state and SEC registration due to market fluctuations.

Exempt from SEC registration: advisers whose only clients are insurance companies, advisers to venture capital funds, advisers to private funds with less than $150 million AUM, foreign private advisers with fewer than 15 U.S. clients and less than $25 million U.S. AUM, and advisers registered with the CFTC. State exemptions vary but commonly include advisers with no place of business in the state and fewer than 5 clients (the 'de minimis' exemption), and advisers whose only clients are institutions, broker-dealers, or other investment advisers.

Form ADV is the registration document for investment advisers. Part 1 contains information about the adviser's business, ownership, clients, employees, business practices, affiliations, and disciplinary history. Part 2A (the 'brochure') is a narrative document describing the adviser's services, fees, investment strategies, conflicts of interest, disciplinary information, and other material facts. Part 2B (the 'brochure supplement') provides information about specific advisory personnel who provide advice to the client. The brochure must be delivered to clients before or at the time of entering into an advisory contract, and annually thereafter (or a summary of material changes with an offer to deliver the full brochure).

Investment advisers owe a fiduciary duty to their clients, established by the Supreme Court in SEC v. Capital Gains Research Bureau (1963). This duty has two primary components: the duty of loyalty (placing the client's interests ahead of the adviser's own interests) and the duty of care (providing advice with competence and diligence). The fiduciary duty is ongoing — it applies at all times during the advisory relationship, not just at the point of recommendation.

Specific fiduciary obligations include: full disclosure of all material conflicts of interest, obtaining best execution for client transactions, ensuring recommendations are suitable for the client's individual circumstances, maintaining the confidentiality of client information, and not engaging in self-dealing without informed client consent. The adviser must disclose compensation arrangements (fees, commissions, revenue sharing), affiliations with broker-dealers or other financial institutions, and any financial condition that could impair the adviser's ability to meet contractual obligations.

Prohibited practices under the Uniform Securities Act include: engaging in dishonest or unethical business practices, making untrue statements of material fact or omitting material facts, engaging in market manipulation (wash trades, matched orders, painting the tape), front-running (trading ahead of client orders), churning (excessive trading to generate commissions), and guaranteeing against loss. An adviser may not charge performance-based fees to retail clients unless the client is a 'qualified client' (net worth exceeding $2.2 million or AUM with the adviser exceeding $1.1 million).

Advisory contracts must be in writing and must include: the services to be provided, the term of the contract, the advisory fee (including the formula for computing the fee), the amount of any prepaid fee and the refund formula if the contract is terminated early, whether the contract grants discretionary authority, and a statement that the contract cannot be assigned without client consent. Assignment includes any transfer of the contract and any change in the controlling ownership of the adviser.

Fee structures include: asset-based fees (percentage of AUM, typically 0.50-1.50% annually), hourly fees, fixed fees, performance-based fees (only for qualified clients), and wrap fees (a single fee covering advisory services, brokerage, and custody). The adviser must disclose all compensation received, including soft-dollar arrangements (receiving research or other benefits from broker-dealers in exchange for directing client trades). Soft-dollar arrangements are permissible under Section 28(e) of the Securities Exchange Act if the benefits are used for research that assists in the investment decision-making process.

Under the Uniform Securities Act, securities must be registered before they can be offered or sold in a state. Three registration methods are available: registration by coordination (used when the security is also being registered with the SEC under the Securities Act of 1933 — the state registration becomes effective simultaneously with the federal registration), registration by qualification (used for securities not being registered federally — requires the most extensive disclosure and is effective only when the state administrator orders it), and registration by filing (notification) (used for established issuers meeting specific criteria — the simplest method, effective upon filing with the state).

Securities exempt from state registration include: U.S. government and agency securities, municipal securities, bank and savings institution securities, insurance company securities (regulated by state insurance departments), securities listed on major exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ), investment company securities registered under the Investment Company Act of 1940, and securities issued by nonprofit organizations. Exempt transactions include: isolated non-issuer transactions (ordinary secondary market trades), transactions between issuers and underwriters, institutional transactions, and private placements to no more than 10 non-institutional purchasers in a 12-month period.

The state securities administrator has broad enforcement powers: issue cease and desist orders, deny, suspend, or revoke registrations of securities, broker-dealers, agents, investment advisers, and investment adviser representatives. The administrator can impose civil penalties and refer criminal matters to the attorney general. Grounds for denial, suspension, or revocation include: filing an incomplete or misleading application, being convicted of a securities-related felony (within the past 10 years) or any felony (within the past 10 years), being subject to an SEC or FINRA disciplinary order, being insolvent, engaging in dishonest or unethical business practices, and failing to reasonably supervise agents or employees.

The statute of limitations for civil liability under the USA is: the earlier of 2 years after discovery of the violation or 3 years after the sale (for fraud or misrepresentation). Buyers who purchased securities based on material misstatements or omissions can sue for rescission (return of the purchase price plus interest, minus any income received) or damages (the difference between the purchase price and the value at the time of the lawsuit). The burden of proof is on the seller to show they did not know and could not reasonably have known of the misstatement.

Broker-dealers must register in each state where they conduct business. Agents (registered representatives) must register in the state where they have a place of business and in each state where they conduct business with non-institutional clients. Agents must be associated with a registered broker-dealer or issuer. An agent's registration is automatically suspended if the BD's registration is revoked or suspended. Agents must pass appropriate qualification exams (SIE + Series 7 for general securities, SIE + Series 6 for investment company products).

Continuing education requirements apply to all registered persons. FINRA's CE program has two elements: the Regulatory Element (computer-based training required within 120 days of the second registration anniversary and every 3 years thereafter — failure to complete results in automatic suspension) and the Firm Element (annual training developed by the firm based on its business activities and regulatory developments). The Series 66 exam itself requires the SIE as a co-requisite — candidates must pass both the SIE and the Series 66 to become registered as an investment adviser representative.

Agents must be associated with a registered broker-dealer or issuer. An agent's registration is automatically suspended if the BD's registration is revoked or suspended. Agents must pass appropriate qualification exams (SIE + Series 7 for general securities, SIE + Series 6 for investment company products).

Continuing education requirements apply to all registered persons. FINRA's CE program has two elements: the Regulatory Element (computer-based training required within 120 days of the second registration anniversary and every 3 years thereafter — failure to complete results in automatic suspension) and the Firm Element (annual training developed by the firm based on its business activities and regulatory developments). The Series 66 exam itself requires the SIE as a co-requisite — candidates must pass both the SIE and the Series 66 to become registered as an investment adviser representative.

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), developed by Harry Markowitz, demonstrates that an optimal portfolio can be constructed to maximize expected return for a given level of risk (or minimize risk for a given return) through diversification. The efficient frontier represents the set of portfolios that offer the highest expected return for each level of risk. Portfolios below the efficient frontier are suboptimal because they offer lower returns for the same risk or higher risk for the same return.

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) extends MPT by introducing the risk-free rate and the market risk premium. Expected Return = Risk-Free Rate + Beta x (Market Return - Risk-Free Rate). The Security Market Line (SML) plots expected return against beta. Securities above the SML are undervalued (positive alpha); securities below are overvalued (negative alpha). The Capital Market Line (CML) plots expected return against total risk (standard deviation) for efficient portfolios only.

Strategic asset allocation establishes long-term target weights based on the client's risk-return profile. Tactical asset allocation makes short-term adjustments to exploit market opportunities or reduce risk. Dynamic asset allocation adjusts the mix as market conditions change (e.g., portfolio insurance strategies). Constant-weighting allocation rebalances periodically to maintain target weights. The choice of allocation strategy depends on the client's investment horizon, risk tolerance, and the adviser's market outlook.

The Sharpe ratio measures risk-adjusted return using total risk (standard deviation): Sharpe = (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. Higher is better. Appropriate for non-diversified portfolios. The Treynor ratio uses systematic risk (beta): Treynor = (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Beta. Appropriate for diversified portfolios (where unsystematic risk has been eliminated). Jensen's alpha measures the excess return above what CAPM predicts: Alpha = Actual Return - [Risk-Free Rate + Beta x (Market Return - Risk-Free Rate)]. Positive alpha indicates manager skill.

R-squared measures the percentage of portfolio returns explained by the benchmark (0-100). High R-squared (>70) indicates the portfolio closely tracks the benchmark, making Treynor ratio and alpha appropriate measures. Low R-squared indicates significant unsystematic risk, making the Sharpe ratio more appropriate. Standard deviation measures total risk (both systematic and unsystematic). Beta measures only systematic risk. A portfolio with high R-squared and positive alpha suggests a skilled manager who adds value through security selection.

The fiduciary standard requires investment advisers to act in the client's best interest at all times. This is an ongoing duty, not a point-in-time obligation. Key fiduciary requirements: full disclosure of all material conflicts of interest, best execution for client transactions, suitability of recommendations for individual circumstances, confidentiality of client information, and prohibition on self-dealing without informed consent. The duty of loyalty means the adviser must not place their own interests ahead of the client's.

Prohibited practices include: churning (excessive trading to generate fees), front-running (trading ahead of client orders), cherry-picking (allocating favorable trades to preferred accounts), commingling (mixing client and firm assets), unauthorized trading (executing trades without client approval or discretionary authority), and guaranteeing against loss. Soft-dollar arrangements must comply with Section 28(e): the research or services received must benefit the advisory clients, and the arrangement must be fully disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A.

The Investment Advisers Act of 1940 requires advisers to maintain a written code of ethics that addresses: personal trading policies (pre-clearance, reporting, restricted lists), gifts and entertainment (limits on giving and receiving), outside business activities (disclosure and approval), political contributions (pay-to-play rules), and confidentiality of client information. Access persons must report personal securities transactions quarterly and holdings annually. The code must be provided to all supervised persons and acknowledged in writing.

Investment advisers must register with either the SEC or the state, depending on assets under management. SEC registration is required for advisers with $100 million or more in AUM (Form ADV filed through IARD). State registration is required for advisers with less than $100 million in AUM. Advisers with $100-110 million in AUM may choose either SEC or state registration (the buffer zone). Multi-state advisers registered in 15 or more states may register with the SEC regardless of AUM level.

Form ADV has two parts: Part 1 contains information about the adviser's business, ownership, clients, employees, business practices, affiliations, and disciplinary history. Part 2A (the 'brochure') must be delivered to clients and contains: fees, services, investment strategies, risks, disciplinary information, conflicts of interest, and other material information. Part 2B (the 'brochure supplement') provides information about specific individuals who provide advice. Form ADV must be updated annually within 90 days of the fiscal year end and promptly for material changes.

Investment adviser representatives (IARs) are individuals who provide investment advice, manage accounts, or solicit advisory services on behalf of the adviser. IARs must register in the state(s) where they have a place of business. The Series 65 (Uniform Investment Adviser Law Exam) or Series 66 (combined with SIE) is required for IAR registration. Exemptions from IAR registration may apply for certain professionals (lawyers, accountants, engineers, teachers) whose advisory services are incidental to their profession and who do not receive separate compensation for advice.

The Uniform Securities Act requires securities to be registered in each state where they are offered or sold, unless an exemption applies. Registration methods include: coordination (filed simultaneously with federal registration — effective when the federal registration becomes effective), qualification (independent state registration — the most detailed and time-consuming), and notice filing (for federal covered securities — the state is notified but does not review the registration). Federal covered securities (those listed on national exchanges, mutual funds, and Regulation D offerings) are exempt from state registration but may require notice filing and fee payment.

Exempt transactions under the Uniform Securities Act include: isolated non-issuer transactions (ordinary secondary market trades), transactions with institutional investors (banks, insurance companies, investment companies, pension funds), private placements (offers to no more than 10 non-institutional buyers in a 12-month period, with no general solicitation and no commissions paid to non-licensed persons), and unsolicited brokerage transactions (the client initiates the transaction without any recommendation from the broker-dealer). Exempt securities include: U.S. government securities, municipal securities, bank securities, and securities issued by nonprofit organizations.

FINRA Rule 2111 (Suitability) requires that a BD or registered representative have a reasonable basis for believing a recommendation is suitable for the customer based on the customer's investment profile. Three types of suitability: reasonable-basis suitability (the recommendation is suitable for at least some investors based on the BD's understanding of the product), customer-specific suitability (the recommendation is suitable for this particular customer based on their profile), and quantitative suitability (the series of recommended transactions is not excessive in light of the customer's profile — anti-churning).

The customer's investment profile includes: age, other investments, financial situation and needs, tax status, investment objectives (capital preservation, income, growth, speculation), investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and any other information the customer may disclose. Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) elevates the standard for BDs beyond traditional suitability: the BD must act in the retail customer's best interest at the time of the recommendation, without placing the BD's financial or other interest ahead of the customer's interest. Reg BI's four component obligations are: Disclosure, Care, Conflict of Interest, and Compliance.

Investment objectives exist on a spectrum from most conservative to most aggressive: capital preservation (protect principal, accept lowest returns), income (generate regular cash flow), growth and income (balanced approach), growth (capital appreciation, accept moderate risk), and speculation (maximum returns, accept highest risk including potential total loss). The adviser must ensure that recommendations align with the stated objectives and that the client understands the risks involved. Unsuitable recommendations can result in FINRA disciplinary action, arbitration awards, and regulatory sanctions.
