How to Use This Manual

Estate planning is the process of arranging for the management and transfer of a person's wealth during life and at death, in a manner that minimizes taxes, avoids unnecessary legal complications, and ensures that the client's wishes are carried out. This manual covers the essential concepts tested on estate planning examinations and provides the foundation for the estate planning component of the CFP exam. Estate planning integrates knowledge from tax law, property law, trust law, insurance, and financial planning into a comprehensive strategy.

The 2026 estate planning landscape is shaped by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, which permanently set the federal estate and gift tax exemption at $15 million per person ($30 million for married couples using portability), indexed for inflation beginning in 2027. The top estate and gift tax rate remains 40%. These permanent provisions eliminate the uncertainty that existed under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act's temporary provisions and allow for long-term planning with confidence.

Understanding property ownership is fundamental to estate planning because the form of ownership determines how property is transferred at death. Fee simple (sole ownership) gives the owner complete control and the property passes through the will or intestacy laws. Tenancy in common (TIC) allows multiple owners to hold undivided interests (which may be unequal) that pass through each owner's estate at death. Joint tenancy with right of survivorship (JTWROS) automatically transfers the deceased owner's interest to the surviving owner(s), bypassing probate. Tenancy by the entirety is a form of JTWROS available only to married couples in certain states, providing additional creditor protection.

Community property (recognized in 9 states plus the District of Columbia) treats all property acquired during marriage as owned equally by both spouses, regardless of which spouse earned the income. Separate property includes assets owned before marriage, gifts, and inheritances. At death, each spouse can dispose of their half of community property. Community property receives a full step-up in basis at the first spouse's death (both halves), unlike JTWROS property where only the deceased owner's half receives a step-up.

Probate is the court-supervised process of validating a will, inventorying assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing remaining assets to beneficiaries. Probate is public (anyone can access the records), potentially expensive (attorney fees, executor fees, and court costs can total 2-7% of the estate value), and time-consuming (typically 6-18 months, longer for complex or contested estates). Assets that bypass probate include: property held in JTWROS or tenancy by the entirety, assets with beneficiary designations (life insurance, retirement accounts, TOD/POD accounts), assets held in trust, and community property with right of survivorship.

Intestacy occurs when a person dies without a valid will. State intestacy laws determine how assets are distributed, typically to the surviving spouse and children in specified shares. If there are no surviving relatives, the estate escheats (reverts) to the state. Intestacy results in the court appointing an administrator (rather than the decedent choosing an executor), potentially higher costs, and distribution that may not match the decedent's wishes. Every estate plan should include, at minimum, a will, durable power of attorney, healthcare proxy, and living will.

A revocable living trust is the cornerstone of many estate plans. The grantor creates the trust, transfers assets into it, serves as trustee during life, and names successor trustees and beneficiaries. Because the grantor retains control, the trust assets are included in the taxable estate and the trust is a grantor trust for income tax purposes (all income is reported on the grantor's personal return). The primary benefits are probate avoidance, privacy, incapacity planning (the successor trustee manages assets if the grantor becomes incapacitated), and continuity of asset management.

Irrevocable trusts remove assets from the grantor's taxable estate because the grantor relinquishes all control. The transfer to an irrevocable trust is a completed gift, potentially subject to gift tax. Common irrevocable trusts include: Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) — holds life insurance outside the estate (subject to the 3-year lookback rule for transferred policies); Credit Shelter Trust (CST) / Bypass Trust — preserves the deceased spouse's exemption amount while providing income to the surviving spouse; Qualified Personal Residence Trust (QPRT) — transfers a home at a discounted gift tax value; and Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) — transfers appreciating assets at reduced gift tax cost.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) provide income to the donor (or other non-charitable beneficiary) for life or a term of years, with the remainder going to charity. The donor receives an immediate income tax deduction for the present value of the charitable remainder interest. A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) pays a fixed dollar amount annually. A Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT) pays a fixed percentage of the trust's annually revalued assets. CRTs are tax-exempt entities — they pay no income tax on gains from selling appreciated assets, making them excellent vehicles for diversifying concentrated stock positions.

Charitable Lead Trusts (CLTs) are the reverse of CRTs: they provide income to charity for a specified period, with the remainder going to family members. The gift/estate tax value of the remainder interest is reduced by the present value of the charitable income stream. Special Needs Trusts (SNTs) provide supplemental support for disabled beneficiaries without disqualifying them from government benefits (Medicaid, SSI). Spendthrift trusts include provisions preventing beneficiaries from assigning their interests and protecting trust assets from beneficiaries' creditors.

The federal gift tax applies to transfers of property during life for less than full consideration. The annual gift tax exclusion for 2026 is $19,000 per donee ($38,000 for married couples using gift-splitting). Gifts within the annual exclusion do not reduce the lifetime exemption and do not require a gift tax return (unless gift-splitting is elected, which requires Form 709). The lifetime gift and estate tax exemption is $15 million per person (2026, OBBBA). Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the lifetime exemption dollar-for-dollar. The unlimited marital deduction allows unlimited gifts between U.S. citizen spouses. For non-citizen spouses, the annual exclusion is $185,000 (2026). Gifts to qualified charities are fully deductible with no limit.

Present interest gifts qualify for the annual exclusion. Future interest gifts (where the donee cannot immediately use or enjoy the property) do not qualify. Gifts to irrevocable trusts are generally future interest gifts unless the trust includes Crummey withdrawal powers, which give beneficiaries a temporary right to withdraw contributions, converting them to present interest gifts eligible for the annual exclusion. The 5-year gift tax averaging provision for 529 plans allows a lump-sum contribution of up to $95,000 ($190,000 for couples) to be treated as if made over 5 years for gift tax purposes.

The federal estate tax applies to the transfer of property at death. The gross estate includes all assets owned by the decedent at death (at fair market value), plus life insurance proceeds on policies owned by the decedent, the decedent's share of jointly held property, property over which the decedent held a general power of appointment, and certain transfers made within 3 years of death (including life insurance policies transferred to an ILIT). The taxable estate equals the gross estate minus deductions for debts, funeral expenses, administration costs, the marital deduction (unlimited for U.S. citizen spouses), and the charitable deduction (unlimited).

The estate tax exemption is $15 million per person (2026). Portability allows the surviving spouse to use the deceased spouse's unused exemption amount (DSUE), effectively doubling the exemption to $30 million for married couples. To elect portability, the executor must file a timely estate tax return (Form 706), even if no tax is due. The step-up in basis at death resets the cost basis of inherited assets to fair market value, permanently eliminating unrealized capital gains. This is one of the most significant tax benefits in the entire tax code.

Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs) allow the grantor to transfer appreciating assets to beneficiaries at a reduced gift tax cost. The grantor contributes assets to the trust and retains an annuity payment for a specified term. The gift tax value is the value of the contributed assets minus the present value of the retained annuity (calculated using the Section 7520 rate). If the assets appreciate faster than the 7520 rate, the excess passes to beneficiaries gift-tax-free. A zeroed-out GRAT sets the annuity payments equal to the full value of the contributed assets, resulting in a gift tax value of approximately zero.

Family Limited Partnerships (FLPs) and Family LLCs allow parents to transfer business or investment assets to children at discounted values. The parents contribute assets to the entity and gift limited partnership interests to children. Because limited partners lack control and marketability, the interests are valued at a discount (typically 20-40% combined discount for lack of control and lack of marketability). The IRS scrutinizes FLPs closely, and the entity must have a legitimate business purpose beyond tax reduction. Installment sales to Intentionally Defective Grantor Trusts (IDGTs) allow the grantor to sell appreciating assets to the trust in exchange for a promissory note, freezing the value for estate tax purposes while the grantor continues to pay income tax on the trust's income (further reducing the estate).

Business succession planning ensures the orderly transfer of a business at the owner's retirement, disability, or death. Buy-sell agreements are the cornerstone of business succession planning. A cross-purchase agreement has the remaining owners buy the departing owner's interest (each owner owns a policy on the other owners). An entity purchase (stock redemption) agreement has the business itself buy the departing owner's interest. Cross-purchase agreements provide a stepped-up basis to the purchasing owners; entity purchases do not. For businesses with many owners, an entity purchase is simpler (the business owns one policy per owner rather than each owner owning policies on every other owner).

Key person insurance protects the business against the financial loss resulting from the death or disability of a key employee. The business owns the policy, pays the premiums (not deductible), and receives the death benefit (tax-free). Section 303 stock redemptions allow a corporation to redeem stock from a deceased shareholder's estate to pay estate taxes and administration expenses, with the redemption treated as a sale (capital gain) rather than a dividend (ordinary income). Section 6166 allows estates consisting largely of closely held business interests to pay estate taxes in installments over up to 14 years.

The ILIT is one of the most powerful estate planning tools for high-net-worth individuals. The trust is established as an irrevocable trust that owns a life insurance policy on the grantor's life. Because the trust owns the policy (not the insured), the death benefit is excluded from the insured's taxable estate. The trustee applies for and owns the policy, pays premiums using gifts from the grantor, and distributes the death benefit to beneficiaries according to the trust terms.

Crummey withdrawal powers are essential for ILIT funding. When the grantor makes a gift to the ILIT to pay premiums, each beneficiary receives a written notice of their right to withdraw their share of the gift (typically for 30-60 days). This converts the gift from a future interest (which does not qualify for the annual exclusion) to a present interest (which does qualify). The annual exclusion ($19,000 per beneficiary in 2026) allows the grantor to fund the ILIT without using any lifetime exemption, provided there are enough beneficiaries.

The 3-year lookback rule (IRC Section 2035) provides that if the insured transfers an existing life insurance policy to an ILIT and dies within 3 years of the transfer, the death benefit is included in the insured's estate. To avoid this risk, the best practice is to have the ILIT trustee apply for and purchase a new policy rather than transferring an existing policy. If an existing policy must be transferred, the grantor must survive 3 years for the estate exclusion to be effective.

A GRAT is an irrevocable trust in which the grantor transfers assets and retains the right to receive an annuity payment for a specified term of years. At the end of the term, the remaining trust assets pass to the beneficiaries (or remain in trust for their benefit). The gift tax value of the transfer is the value of the assets contributed minus the present value of the retained annuity interest (calculated using the Section 7520 rate, which is 120% of the mid-term AFR). If the assets appreciate faster than the 7520 rate, the excess appreciation passes to beneficiaries free of gift and estate tax.

A zeroed-out GRAT sets the annuity payments equal to the full value of the contributed assets (plus the 7520 rate of return), resulting in a gift tax value of approximately zero. This means the grantor can transfer potentially unlimited appreciation to beneficiaries without using any lifetime exemption. The risk: if the grantor dies during the GRAT term, the trust assets are included in the grantor's estate (the strategy fails, but the estate is no worse off). GRATs work best with assets expected to appreciate significantly (concentrated stock positions, pre-IPO shares) and when the 7520 rate is low.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) come in two forms: the Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) pays a fixed annuity (at least 5% of the initial fair market value) to the income beneficiary. No additional contributions are permitted. The Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT) pays a fixed percentage (at least 5%) of the trust's annually revalued assets. Additional contributions are permitted. Both provide an immediate income tax deduction for the present value of the charitable remainder interest (which must be at least 10% of the initial contribution).

CRTs are particularly effective for diversifying concentrated stock positions. The client contributes highly appreciated stock to the CRT. The CRT sells the stock without paying capital gains tax (CRTs are tax-exempt entities). The proceeds are reinvested in a diversified portfolio. The client receives income for life (or a term of years), and the charity receives the remainder. Without the CRT, selling the stock directly would trigger significant capital gains tax, reducing the amount available for reinvestment.

Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs) provide a simpler alternative to private foundations for charitable giving. The donor makes an irrevocable contribution to the DAF (receiving an immediate tax deduction), then recommends grants to charities over time. DAFs have no minimum distribution requirement (unlike private foundations, which must distribute 5% annually), no excise tax on investment income, and minimal administrative burden. The donor retains advisory privileges but not legal control over the funds.

The federal gift tax applies to transfers of property during life for less than full and adequate consideration. The annual exclusion allows each donor to give up to $19,000 per recipient per year (2026) without gift tax or using any lifetime exemption. Married couples can split gifts, effectively doubling the exclusion to $38,000 per recipient. Gifts to a spouse who is a U.S. citizen qualify for the unlimited marital deduction. Gifts to a non-citizen spouse are limited to $185,000 annually (2026).

The lifetime gift and estate tax exemption is unified at $15 million per individual in 2026 (under the OBBBA extension). Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the lifetime exemption dollar-for-dollar. The gift tax rate is 40% on amounts exceeding the exemption. Certain transfers are not considered taxable gifts: payments made directly to educational institutions for tuition (not room and board), payments made directly to medical providers for medical expenses, transfers to political organizations, and transfers to qualifying charities.

Advanced gifting strategies include: annual exclusion gifting programs (systematic gifts to multiple family members to transfer wealth over time), Crummey trusts (irrevocable trusts with withdrawal powers that convert future interest gifts into present interest gifts qualifying for the annual exclusion), 529 plan superfunding (contributing 5 years of annual exclusions in a single year — $95,000 per beneficiary in 2026), and grantor trust strategies (the grantor pays income tax on trust income, effectively making additional tax-free gifts to the trust beneficiaries).

The GSTT is a separate tax imposed on transfers that skip a generation — transfers to grandchildren, great-grandchildren, or unrelated individuals more than 37.5 years younger than the transferor. The GSTT rate is a flat 40% (the highest estate tax rate), applied in addition to any gift or estate tax. The GSTT exemption is $15 million per individual in 2026 (same as the estate tax exemption). Proper allocation of the GSTT exemption is critical to avoid the devastating impact of double taxation (estate tax plus GSTT).

There are three types of generation-skipping transfers: direct skips (transfers directly to a skip person, such as a gift to a grandchild), taxable distributions (distributions from a trust to a skip person), and taxable terminations (when the interest of a non-skip person in a trust terminates and only skip persons remain as beneficiaries). The GSTT exemption can be allocated to trusts to create 'GST-exempt' trusts that can benefit multiple generations without further transfer tax. Dynasty trusts leverage this by lasting for multiple generations in states that have abolished the rule against perpetuities.

A durable power of attorney (DPOA) authorizes an agent to act on behalf of the principal for financial matters. 'Durable' means the authority continues even if the principal becomes incapacitated (a non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity). A springing DPOA becomes effective only upon the principal's incapacity (as certified by one or more physicians). A general DPOA grants broad authority over all financial matters; a limited (special) DPOA grants authority only for specific transactions or time periods.

A healthcare power of attorney (healthcare proxy) authorizes an agent to make medical decisions when the principal cannot. A living will (advance directive) expresses the principal's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment, artificial nutrition and hydration, and organ donation. A HIPAA authorization allows designated individuals to access the principal's protected health information. These documents should be reviewed and updated regularly, especially after life events such as marriage, divorce, or relocation to a different state.

Business succession planning ensures the orderly transfer of business ownership upon the owner's death, disability, or retirement. Buy-sell agreements are the cornerstone of business succession: they establish a predetermined price and terms for the transfer of business interests. Cross-purchase agreements allow surviving owners to purchase the deceased owner's interest (funded by life insurance policies each owner holds on the others). Entity-purchase (stock redemption) agreements allow the business entity itself to purchase the deceased owner's interest (funded by entity-owned life insurance).

For partnerships with N partners, a cross-purchase requires N x (N-1) policies, while an entity-purchase requires only N policies. For example, a 4-partner firm needs 12 policies under cross-purchase but only 4 under entity-purchase. However, cross-purchase provides a stepped-up basis to the surviving partners (the purchase price becomes their new basis), while entity-purchase does not increase the surviving partners' basis in their partnership interests. A Section 754 election can mitigate this disadvantage for entity-purchase agreements.

Valuation methods for buy-sell agreements include: book value (simple but may not reflect true value), capitalization of earnings (applies a capitalization rate to normalized earnings), discounted cash flow (projects future cash flows and discounts to present value), and fair market value determined by independent appraisal. The agreed-upon valuation in a buy-sell agreement can establish the estate tax value of the business interest if the agreement meets certain requirements: it must be a bona fide business arrangement, not a device to transfer property to family members for less than adequate consideration, and the terms must be comparable to similar arm's-length arrangements.

A Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) provides income to the donor (or other non-charitable beneficiaries) for life or a term of years, with the remainder passing to charity. The donor receives an immediate income tax deduction for the present value of the charitable remainder interest. CRTs are exempt from income tax, so assets can be sold within the trust without triggering capital gains tax — making them ideal for highly appreciated assets. A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) pays a fixed annuity amount (at least 5% of initial trust value). A Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT) pays a fixed percentage (at least 5%) of the trust value as revalued annually.

A Charitable Lead Trust (CLT) is the reverse: charity receives income for a specified period, with the remainder passing to non-charitable beneficiaries (typically family members). CLTs are effective for transferring wealth to the next generation at a reduced gift/estate tax cost. A Charitable Lead Annuity Trust (CLAT) pays a fixed annuity to charity — if the trust assets grow faster than the IRS Section 7520 rate used to calculate the gift tax value, the excess passes to the remainder beneficiaries gift-tax-free. Donor-advised funds (DAFs) offer a simpler alternative: the donor makes an irrevocable contribution, receives an immediate deduction, and recommends grants to charities over time.

Critical 2026 estate planning numbers: Federal estate/gift tax exemption: $15 million per individual ($30 million married with portability). Annual gift tax exclusion: $19,000 per recipient ($38,000 with gift splitting). GSTT exemption: $15 million per individual. Maximum estate/gift/GSTT tax rate: 40%. Gift to non-citizen spouse annual exclusion: $185,000. Charitable deduction: unlimited for estate tax purposes. Marital deduction: unlimited for U.S. citizen spouses. These numbers are indexed for inflation and may change annually.

The stepped-up basis rule (IRC Section 1014) adjusts the cost basis of inherited property to its fair market value at the date of death. This eliminates all unrealized capital gains that accrued during the decedent's lifetime. For example, if the decedent purchased stock for $10,000 and it was worth $100,000 at death, the heir's basis is $100,000 — if sold immediately, there is no capital gain. This is one of the most significant tax benefits in the tax code and is a key consideration in estate planning: highly appreciated assets should generally be held until death rather than gifted during life (gifts carry over the donor's basis).

Estate planning is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. Plans should be reviewed at least every 3-5 years and whenever a significant life event occurs: marriage, divorce, birth of a child or grandchild, death of a beneficiary, significant change in assets, change in state of residence, or changes in tax law. The 2026 estate tax exemption of $15 million is scheduled to sunset after 2025 under current law projections, potentially reverting to approximately $7 million (adjusted for inflation). Clients with estates between $7 million and $15 million should plan now to take advantage of the current higher exemption through lifetime gifting strategies.

Gift Tax Rules and Strategies

The federal gift tax applies to transfers of property during life for less than full and adequate consideration. The annual exclusion allows each donor to give up to $19,000 per recipient per year (2026) without gift tax or using any lifetime exemption. Married couples can split gifts, effectively doubling the exclusion to $38,000 per recipient. Gifts to a spouse who is a U.S. citizen qualify for the unlimited marital deduction. Gifts to a non-citizen spouse are limited to $185,000 annually (2026).

The lifetime gift and estate tax exemption is unified at $15 million per individual in 2026 (under the OBBBA extension). Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the lifetime exemption dollar-for-dollar. The gift tax rate is 40% on amounts exceeding the exemption. Certain transfers are not considered taxable gifts: payments made directly to educational institutions for tuition (not room and board), payments made directly to medical providers for medical expenses, transfers to political organizations, and transfers to qualifying charities.

Advanced gifting strategies include: annual exclusion gifting programs (systematic gifts to multiple family members to transfer wealth over time), Crummey trusts (irrevocable trusts with withdrawal powers that convert future interest gifts into present interest gifts qualifying for the annual exclusion), 529 plan superfunding (contributing 5 years of annual exclusions in a single year — $95,000 per beneficiary in 2026), and grantor trust strategies (the grantor pays income tax on trust income, effectively making additional tax-free gifts to the trust beneficiaries).

Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax (GSTT)

The GSTT is a separate tax imposed on transfers that skip a generation — transfers to grandchildren, great-grandchildren, or unrelated individuals more than 37.5 years younger than the transferor. The GSTT rate is a flat 40% (the highest estate tax rate), applied in addition to any gift or estate tax. The GSTT exemption is $15 million per individual in 2026 (same as the estate tax exemption). Proper allocation of the GSTT exemption is critical to avoid the devastating impact of double taxation (estate tax plus GSTT).

There are three types of generation-skipping transfers: direct skips (transfers directly to a skip person, such as a gift to a grandchild), taxable distributions (distributions from a trust to a skip person), and taxable terminations (when the interest of a non-skip person in a trust terminates and only skip persons remain as beneficiaries). The GSTT exemption can be allocated to trusts to create 'GST-exempt' trusts that can benefit multiple generations without further transfer tax. Dynasty trusts leverage this by lasting for multiple generations in states that have abolished the rule against perpetuities.

Powers of Attorney and Advance Directives

A durable power of attorney (DPOA) authorizes an agent to act on behalf of the principal for financial matters. 'Durable' means the authority continues even if the principal becomes incapacitated (a non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity). A springing DPOA becomes effective only upon the principal's incapacity (as certified by one or more physicians). A general DPOA grants broad authority over all financial matters; a limited (special) DPOA grants authority only for specific transactions or time periods.

A healthcare power of attorney (healthcare proxy) authorizes an agent to make medical decisions when the principal cannot. A living will (advance directive) expresses the principal's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment, artificial nutrition and hydration, and organ donation. A HIPAA authorization allows designated individuals to access the principal's protected health information. These documents should be reviewed and updated regularly, especially after life events such as marriage, divorce, or relocation to a different state.
