How to Use This Manual

The Investment Advisory Practice manual prepares professionals for the advisory side of the financial services industry. Investment advisers provide personalized investment advice for compensation, and they owe a fiduciary duty to their clients. This manual covers the essential knowledge areas for investment advisory practice: the regulatory framework, portfolio construction and management, client relationship management, and compliance requirements. It complements the Series 66 manual (which focuses on the legal framework) by emphasizing the practical aspects of running an advisory practice.

An investment adviser is any person who, for compensation, engages in the business of advising others about the value of securities or the advisability of investing in securities. The three-prong test requires: (1) providing advice about securities, (2) being in the business of providing such advice, and (3) receiving compensation. All three must be met. Exclusions from the definition include banks, lawyers, accountants, engineers, and teachers (whose advice is incidental to their profession), broker-dealers (whose advice is incidental to brokerage services and who receive no special compensation for advice), publishers of general circulation publications, and government securities advisers.

The fiduciary duty is the cornerstone of the advisory relationship. Unlike broker-dealers (who are subject to Regulation Best Interest at the time of recommendation), investment advisers must act in the client's best interest at ALL times. This includes the duty of loyalty (placing the client's interests above the adviser's own), the duty of care (providing advice with competence and thoroughness), and the duty to follow client instructions. The adviser must disclose all material conflicts of interest, obtain informed consent before engaging in conflicted transactions, and seek best execution for client trades.

Fee structures include assets under management (AUM) fees (typically 0.50-1.50% annually, aligning the adviser's interests with the client's), hourly fees ($150-$500/hour), flat fees ($1,000-$10,000 per plan), and performance-based fees (permitted only for qualified clients with $1.1 million+ in AUM or $2.2 million+ net worth). Commission-based compensation creates conflicts of interest because the adviser may be incentivized to recommend products that generate higher commissions rather than those that are best for the client. Fee-only advisers avoid this conflict but may have other conflicts (such as incentives to gather more AUM).

The Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is the foundation of the advisory relationship. It documents the client's return objectives, risk tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, tax situation, legal constraints, and unique circumstances. The IPS guides all investment decisions and provides a benchmark for evaluating performance. It should be reviewed and updated at least annually or whenever the client's circumstances change significantly.

Modern Portfolio Theory demonstrates that diversification with imperfectly correlated assets reduces portfolio risk. The efficient frontier represents portfolios offering the highest expected return for each level of risk. Strategic asset allocation establishes long-term target weights. Tactical asset allocation allows short-term deviations. Core-satellite combines passive core holdings (index funds) with active satellite positions. Factor-based investing targets specific risk factors (value, size, momentum, quality) that have historically generated excess returns.

Portfolio rebalancing returns the portfolio to target weights after market movements cause drift. Calendar rebalancing occurs at fixed intervals (quarterly, semi-annually, annually). Threshold rebalancing occurs when any asset class drifts beyond a specified tolerance band (e.g., +/- 5% from target). Tax-efficient rebalancing uses new contributions, dividend reinvestment, and tax-loss harvesting to minimize the tax impact of rebalancing trades. Performance measurement compares portfolio returns against appropriate benchmarks using risk-adjusted measures (Sharpe ratio, Treynor ratio, Jensen's alpha, information ratio).

Effective client communication is essential for building trust and maintaining long-term relationships. The initial discovery meeting establishes the client's goals, values, and financial situation. Subsequent meetings should review progress, discuss market conditions, and address any changes in the client's circumstances. Communication should be clear, jargon-free, and tailored to the client's level of financial sophistication. Written reports should include portfolio performance, asset allocation, and progress toward goals.

Behavioral finance insights help advisers manage client emotions during market volatility. Loss aversion causes clients to feel losses twice as intensely as equivalent gains. Recency bias causes clients to overweight recent market events. Anchoring causes fixation on irrelevant reference points (purchase prices, all-time highs). The adviser's role is to help clients maintain discipline, avoid emotional decisions, and stay focused on long-term goals. Regular communication during market downturns is particularly important to prevent panic selling.

Investment advisers must register with the SEC (if AUM exceeds $100 million) or the state (if AUM is below $100 million). Form ADV is the registration document: Part 1 contains firm information filed with regulators; Part 2A (the brochure) describes services, fees, strategies, and conflicts; Part 2B (the brochure supplement) describes the individuals providing advice. The brochure must be delivered to clients before or at the time of entering the advisory contract and updated annually. Material changes must be communicated to clients within 120 days of the fiscal year end.

Compliance requirements include: maintaining books and records (for 5 years, 2 years in the principal office), adopting a written compliance program, designating a Chief Compliance Officer (CCO), conducting annual compliance reviews, maintaining a code of ethics (including personal trading policies), and implementing policies to prevent insider trading. Custody rules require advisers who have custody of client assets to maintain them with a qualified custodian, provide quarterly account statements, and undergo an annual surprise examination by an independent public accountant.

Advertising rules prohibit testimonials (with exceptions under the SEC's updated marketing rule), guarantees of future performance, and misleading statements. Performance advertising must include appropriate disclosures, use consistent methodology, and present net-of-fees returns. Social media posts are considered advertisements and must comply with the same rules. Solicitation arrangements (paying third parties to refer clients) require written agreements, disclosure to the referred client, and compliance with the SEC's marketing rule.

The investment advisory business model centers on providing ongoing, fiduciary-level advice for a recurring fee. The most common fee structure is asset-based (0.50-1.50% of AUM annually), aligning adviser and client interests. Alternative structures include hourly fees ($150-$400/hour), fixed retainers ($2,000-$10,000/year), and financial planning fees ($1,000-$5,000 per plan). Behavioral finance recognizes that investors are not always rational and that psychological biases can lead to suboptimal investment decisions. Loss aversion (prospect theory, Kahneman and Tversky) demonstrates that the pain of losing is approximately twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining — investors are more motivated to avoid losses than to achieve equivalent gains. This leads to holding losing positions too long (hoping for recovery) and selling winners too quickly (locking in gains).

Confirmation bias causes investors to seek information that confirms their existing beliefs and ignore contradictory evidence. Anchoring bias causes investors to fixate on a reference point (such as the purchase price) when making decisions, even when that reference point is irrelevant. Recency bias causes investors to overweight recent events and underweight historical data. Herding behavior causes investors to follow the crowd rather than conducting independent analysis, contributing to market bubbles and crashes.

Overconfidence bias leads investors to overestimate their knowledge and ability to predict market movements, resulting in excessive trading and insufficient diversification. Mental accounting causes investors to treat money differently based on its source or intended use (e.g., treating a tax refund as 'found money' and spending it frivolously). The disposition effect (related to loss aversion) causes investors to sell winners and hold losers, which is the opposite of optimal tax management (sell losers for tax-loss harvesting, let winners run).

Alternative investments include asset classes beyond traditional stocks and bonds: real estate (direct ownership, REITs, real estate funds), private equity (venture capital, leveraged buyouts, growth equity), hedge funds (long/short, market neutral, event-driven, global macro), commodities (gold, oil, agricultural products), infrastructure (toll roads, airports, utilities), and digital assets. Alternatives can provide diversification benefits due to low correlation with traditional asset classes, but they typically have higher fees, lower liquidity, and less transparency.

Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) are companies that own, operate, or finance income-producing real estate. To qualify as a REIT: at least 75% of assets must be real estate, at least 75% of gross income must come from real estate, and at least 90% of taxable income must be distributed to shareholders as dividends. REIT dividends are generally taxed as ordinary income (not qualified dividends), but the 20% qualified business income (QBI) deduction under Section 199A may apply, effectively reducing the tax rate.

Hedge funds are private investment pools available only to accredited investors and qualified purchasers. They use strategies not typically available to mutual funds: short selling, leverage, derivatives, concentrated positions, and illiquid investments. Common hedge fund fee structures: '2 and 20' (2% management fee plus 20% performance fee on profits above a hurdle rate). Hedge funds have lock-up periods (typically 1-3 years) during which investors cannot redeem their investment. Due diligence is critical: evaluate the manager's track record, strategy, risk management, operational infrastructure, and regulatory compliance.

Form ADV is the primary registration and disclosure document for investment advisers. Part 1 is filed electronically through IARD and contains: identifying information, form of organization, ownership and control persons, advisory activities, compensation arrangements, types of clients, participation in client transactions, custody of client assets, financial information, and disciplinary history. Part 1 is primarily used by regulators for oversight and is publicly available through the SEC's Investment Adviser Public Disclosure (IAPD) database.

Part 2A (the Firm Brochure) must be written in plain English and delivered to prospective clients before or at the time of entering into an advisory agreement, and annually thereafter (either delivering the updated brochure or a summary of material changes). Part 2A must disclose: advisory services offered, fees and compensation, types of clients, methods of analysis and investment strategies, material risks, disciplinary information, other financial industry activities, code of ethics, brokerage practices, review of accounts, client referrals, custody arrangements, investment discretion, voting client securities (proxy voting), and financial information.

Part 2B (the Brochure Supplement) provides information about specific individuals who provide investment advice to clients: educational background, business experience, disciplinary information, other business activities, additional compensation, and supervision. Part 2B must be delivered to clients for each supervised person who provides advice. Form CRS (Client Relationship Summary) is a brief document (2-4 pages) that summarizes the relationship and services, fees, conflicts of interest, legal standard of conduct, and disciplinary history. Required for both BDs and IAs serving retail investors.

The SEC Custody Rule (Rule 206(4)-2) imposes strict requirements on advisers who have custody of client assets. Custody includes: holding client funds or securities, having authority to obtain client funds or securities (e.g., ability to deduct fees from client accounts), and having any capacity that gives the adviser legal ownership or access to client funds or securities. Advisers with custody must: maintain client assets with a qualified custodian (bank, BD, futures commission merchant), have a reasonable basis for believing the custodian sends account statements directly to clients at least quarterly, and undergo an annual surprise examination by an independent public accountant.

The qualified custodian must be independent of the adviser. Client assets must be held in accounts in the client's name (or the adviser's name as agent for the client). The adviser must notify clients of the custodian's name, address, and the manner in which assets are held. If the adviser or a related person serves as the qualified custodian, additional requirements apply, including an annual audit of the custodian by an independent public accountant. Advisers who only have custody because they deduct advisory fees from client accounts are exempt from the surprise examination requirement if they meet certain conditions.

Fixed income portfolio strategies include: buy-and-hold (purchase bonds and hold to maturity — eliminates interest rate risk if held to maturity), laddering (staggering maturities across multiple years to reduce reinvestment risk and provide regular liquidity), barbelling (concentrating holdings in short-term and long-term maturities with little in the middle — provides liquidity from short-term bonds and higher yields from long-term bonds), and bulleting (concentrating maturities around a single target date — used for liability matching).

Duration is the key measure of interest rate sensitivity. Modified duration estimates the percentage price change for a 1% change in yield: Price Change ≈ -Modified Duration x Yield Change. A bond with a duration of 5 will decline approximately 5% if yields rise 1%. Convexity measures the curvature of the price-yield relationship — bonds with positive convexity benefit more from falling rates than they lose from rising rates. Callable bonds have negative convexity at low yields because the call feature caps price appreciation. Immunization matches the duration of assets and liabilities to protect against interest rate changes.

Credit analysis evaluates the issuer's ability and willingness to pay. Key metrics: debt-to-equity ratio (leverage), interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest expense — higher is better), current ratio (current assets / current liabilities — measures short-term liquidity), and free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capital expenditures). Credit spreads (the yield difference between corporate bonds and Treasuries of similar maturity) widen during economic stress and narrow during expansion. Spread analysis helps identify relative value opportunities in the corporate bond market.

Asset location is the strategic placement of investments across account types to minimize taxes. Tax-inefficient investments (bonds generating ordinary income, REITs, actively traded funds with high turnover) should be placed in tax-deferred accounts (IRA, 401(k)) where the income is not currently taxed. Tax-efficient investments (index funds with low turnover, growth stocks held for long-term capital gains, municipal bonds) should be placed in taxable accounts where they benefit from preferential tax rates or tax exemptions.

Tax-loss harvesting involves selling securities at a loss to offset realized capital gains. The wash sale rule prevents claiming the loss if a substantially identical security is purchased within 30 days before or after the sale. Strategies to maintain market exposure while harvesting losses: replace the sold security with a similar (but not substantially identical) fund, use ETFs to replace mutual funds tracking the same index (debatable under wash sale rules), or wait 31 days before repurchasing. Direct indexing (owning individual stocks that replicate an index) provides the most tax-loss harvesting opportunities because individual positions can be sold without triggering wash sales on the entire portfolio.

Risk profiling assesses three dimensions: risk tolerance (the client's emotional comfort with volatility — measured through questionnaires and behavioral observation), risk capacity (the client's financial ability to absorb losses without jeopardizing goals — determined by income stability, time horizon, wealth level, and insurance coverage), and risk need (the level of risk required to achieve the client's goals given their current resources and savings rate). The appropriate risk level is the LOWEST of these three dimensions — even if a client has high tolerance and high capacity, if they don't need risk (already on track), a conservative approach may be appropriate.

The Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is the governing document for the advisory relationship. It should include: client information and background, return objectives (real vs. nominal, total return vs. income), risk tolerance (qualitative description and quantitative measures like maximum drawdown), time horizon (single-stage or multi-stage), liquidity needs (known and potential), tax considerations (marginal rate, loss carryforwards, concentrated positions), legal and regulatory constraints (ERISA, trust provisions, regulatory restrictions), unique circumstances (ESG preferences, concentrated stock positions, restricted securities), and the strategic asset allocation with rebalancing policy.

The IPS should be reviewed at least annually and updated whenever there is a material change in the client's circumstances: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, job change, inheritance, health change, or significant change in financial situation. The IPS serves multiple purposes: it documents the agreed-upon investment strategy (protecting both adviser and client), provides a framework for evaluating performance, establishes rebalancing discipline, and prevents emotional decision-making during market volatility. A well-constructed IPS is the foundation of a successful advisory relationship.

13. The Sharpe ratio is most appropriate for evaluating: A) Diversified portfolios B) Non-diversified portfolios C) Fixed income only D) Equity only. Answer: B. The Sharpe ratio uses total risk (standard deviation) and is appropriate for non-diversified portfolios. The Treynor ratio uses systematic risk (beta) and is appropriate for diversified portfolios.

14. Tax-loss harvesting is limited by: A) The capital gains tax rate B) The wash sale rule C) The alternative minimum tax D) The standard deduction. Answer: B. The wash sale rule prevents claiming a loss if a substantially identical security is purchased within 30 days before or after the sale. This limits the effectiveness of tax-loss harvesting but can be managed by purchasing similar (not identical) securities.

15. Direct indexing provides an advantage over index funds primarily through: A) Lower expense ratios B) Better tracking of the index C) Enhanced tax-loss harvesting opportunities D) Higher returns. Answer: C. Direct indexing (owning individual stocks) allows harvesting losses on individual positions without triggering wash sales on the entire portfolio, providing superior tax-loss harvesting compared to holding a single index fund.

Performance measurement and attribution: Time-weighted return (TWR) measures the portfolio manager's performance independent of cash flows — it is the industry standard for comparing managers. Money-weighted return (MWR/IRR) measures the investor's actual experience including the timing and size of cash flows. The Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS) require TWR for performance reporting. Performance attribution decomposes returns into: asset allocation effect (how much value was added by deviating from the benchmark allocation), security selection effect (how much value was added by choosing specific securities within each asset class), and interaction effect.

Behavioral finance concepts relevant to advisory practice: loss aversion (investors feel losses approximately 2x more intensely than equivalent gains — leads to holding losers too long and selling winners too early), anchoring (relying too heavily on a reference point such as purchase price), confirmation bias (seeking information that confirms existing beliefs), recency bias (overweighting recent events), herding (following the crowd), and overconfidence (overestimating one's ability to pick stocks or time the market). Understanding these biases helps advisers manage client expectations and prevent emotional decision-making during market volatility.

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) investing has moved from niche to mainstream, with advisers increasingly incorporating ESG factors into portfolio construction and client conversations. The Department of Labor's fiduciary rule and the SEC's Regulation Best Interest continue to shape the regulatory landscape, pushing the industry toward higher standards of care for all investors.

Strategies to maintain market exposure while harvesting losses: replace the sold security with a similar (but not substantially identical) fund, use ETFs to replace mutual funds tracking the same index (debatable under wash sale rules), or wait 31 days before repurchasing. Direct indexing (owning individual stocks that replicate an index) provides the most tax-loss harvesting opportunities because individual positions can be sold without triggering wash sales on the entire portfolio.

Client Risk Profiling and IPS Construction

Risk profiling assesses three dimensions: risk tolerance (the client's emotional comfort with volatility — measured through questionnaires and behavioral observation), risk capacity (the client's financial ability to absorb losses without jeopardizing goals — determined by income stability, time horizon, wealth level, and insurance coverage), and risk need (the level of risk required to achieve the client's goals given their current resources and savings rate). The appropriate risk level is the LOWEST of these three dimensions — even if a client has high tolerance and high capacity, if they don't need risk (already on track), a conservative approach may be appropriate.

The Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is the governing document for the advisory relationship. It should include: client information and background, return objectives (real vs. nominal, total return vs. income), risk tolerance (qualitative description and quantitative measures like maximum drawdown), time horizon (single-stage or multi-stage), liquidity needs (known and potential), tax considerations (marginal rate, loss carryforwards, concentrated positions), legal and regulatory constraints (ERISA, trust provisions, regulatory restrictions), unique circumstances (ESG preferences, concentrated stock positions, restricted securities), and the strategic asset allocation with rebalancing policy.

The IPS should be reviewed at least annually and updated whenever there is a material change in the client's circumstances: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, job change, inheritance, health change, or significant change in financial situation. The IPS serves multiple purposes: it documents the agreed-upon investment strategy (protecting both adviser and client), provides a framework for evaluating performance, establishes rebalancing discipline, and prevents emotional decision-making during market volatility. A well-constructed IPS is the foundation of a successful advisory relationship.

Investment Advisory Additional Practice Questions and Review

11. The three dimensions of risk profiling are: A) Risk tolerance, risk capacity, risk need B) Systematic risk, unsystematic risk, total risk C) Market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk D) Standard deviation, beta, alpha. Answer: A. Risk profiling assesses tolerance (emotional comfort), capacity (financial ability to absorb losses), and need (risk required to achieve goals). The appropriate risk level is the lowest of the three.

12. An investment adviser has custody of client assets when: A) The adviser recommends a custodian B) The adviser has authority to deduct fees from client accounts C) The adviser provides performance reports D) The adviser recommends securities. Answer: B. Custody includes holding client assets, having authority to obtain them, or having the ability to deduct fees. Recommending a custodian or providing reports does not constitute custody.

13. The Sharpe ratio is most appropriate for evaluating: A) Diversified portfolios B) Non-diversified portfolios C) Fixed income only D) Equity only. Answer: B. The Sharpe ratio uses total risk (standard deviation) and is appropriate for non-diversified portfolios. The Treynor ratio uses systematic risk (beta) and is appropriate for diversified portfolios.

14. Tax-loss harvesting is limited by: A) The capital gains tax rate B) The wash sale rule C) The alternative minimum tax D) The standard deduction. Answer: B. The wash sale rule prevents claiming a loss if a substantially identical security is purchased within 30 days before or after the sale. This limits the effectiveness of tax-loss harvesting but can be managed by purchasing similar (not identical) securities.

15. Direct indexing provides an advantage over index funds primarily through: A) Lower expense ratios B) Better tracking of the index C) Enhanced tax-loss harvesting opportunities D) Higher returns. Answer: C. Direct indexing (owning individual stocks) allows harvesting losses on individual positions without triggering wash sales on the entire portfolio, providing superior tax-loss harvesting compared to holding a single index fund.

Investment Advisory Final Key Facts

Performance measurement and attribution: Time-weighted return (TWR) measures the portfolio manager's performance independent of cash flows — it is the industry standard for comparing managers. Money-weighted return (MWR/IRR) measures the investor's actual experience including the timing and size of cash flows. The Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS) require TWR for performance reporting. Performance attribution decomposes returns into: asset allocation effect (how much value was added by deviating from the benchmark allocation), security selection effect (how much value was added by choosing specific securities within each asset class), and interaction effect.

Behavioral finance concepts relevant to advisory practice: loss aversion (investors feel losses approximately 2x more intensely than equivalent gains — leads to holding losers too long and selling winners too early), anchoring (relying too heavily on a reference point such as purchase price), confirmation bias (seeking information that confirms existing beliefs), recency bias (overweighting recent events), herding (following the crowd), and overconfidence (overestimating one's ability to pick stocks or time the market). Understanding these biases helps advisers manage client expectations and prevent emotional decision-making during market volatility.
